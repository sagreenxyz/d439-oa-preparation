# Ethics and Legal Topic 3: Advance Directives & End-of-Life Decisions

## Overview: Why Advance Directives Matter

**The fundamental problem they solve:**
Patients get sick or injured and lose the ability to make decisions about their own care. Without guidance, who decides? Family guesses. Doctors guess. Conflicts erupt.

**Advance directives are solutions:** Legal documents created *before* incapacity that tell healthcare providers what the patient wants *if* they can't speak for themselves.

**This embodies the autonomy principle:** Patient's voice speaks even when patient can't speak.

---

## **Two Main Types of Advance Directives**

### **Type 1: LIVING WILL**

#### **What It Is**
A legal document in which a competent person states what medical treatments they do or do not want if they become unable to communicate.

#### **Scope: Applies ONLY When...**
- Patient is **unable to make decisions** (unconscious, severe dementia, permanent coma, etc.)
- AND patient meets the condition specified in the will (usually "terminal illness" or "persistent vegetative state")
- Living wills don't become active just because patient is hospitalized—there's a threshold of incapacity + specific condition

#### **What It Can Address**
- CPR (resuscitation)
- Mechanical ventilation (breathing machine)
- Dialysis
- Tube feeding (artificial nutrition)
- Medications
- Comfort care vs. life-prolonging care
- Organ donation
- Autopsy wishes

#### **How It Works**
Patient fills out form (often state-specific) stating: "If I am in condition X, I do/do not want treatment Y"

**Example language:**
> "If I am diagnosed as terminally ill and expected to die within 6 months, and I become unable to communicate:
> - I do NOT want CPR
> - I do NOT want breathing machine
> - I DO want comfort care and pain medication
> - I DO want tube feeding initially but not if it prolongs suffering"

#### **Legal Status**
- Legally binding document (state law governs specifics)
- Physician is obligated to follow it
- Can be revoked or changed anytime patient is competent

#### **Advantage**
Specific, clear wishes in patient's own words

#### **Disadvantage**
Rigid—doesn't account for situations patient didn't anticipate. Can't adapt to new medical realities.

---

### **Type 2: HEALTHCARE POWER OF ATTORNEY (Medical POA) / Healthcare Proxy**

#### **What It Is**
A legal document naming a specific person (surrogate/proxy) who has authority to make healthcare decisions for the patient if the patient becomes unable to make decisions.

#### **Scope: Applies When...**
- Patient **loses decision-making capacity** (no specific condition threshold required)
- Surrogate then has broad authority to make medical decisions

#### **Key Difference from Living Will**
Surrogate has **FLEXIBILITY** to adapt to situations as they arise, rather than rigid pre-stated wishes.

#### **How The Surrogate Decides**
The surrogate uses a **decision-making hierarchy**:

**Tier 1: Substituted Judgment**
- "What would the patient want if they could decide?"
- Based on values patient shared, conversations, personality
- Most preferred approach

**Tier 2: Best Interest Standard**
- "What is objectively best for this patient's health?"
- Used when patient never expressed wishes
- Less ideal but necessary when no guidance available

**Example:**
Patient named spouse as POA but never discussed wanting CPR or not. Patient has massive stroke with no brain function, on machines. Spouse uses best interest standard to decide: "Given his values of independence and dignity, prolonging this isn't in his best interest."

#### **Legal Status**
- Legally binding once patient loses capacity
- Surrogate has NO authority while patient is competent (unless patient explicitly delegates)
- Can override living will if surrogate believes circumstances are different than anticipated

#### **Advantage**
Flexible; adapts to new situations; gives authority to someone who knows patient

#### **Disadvantage**
Surrogate might not know patient's true wishes; surrogate has personal interests that could bias decisions

---

## **Living Will vs. Healthcare POA: Quick Comparison**

| Feature | Living Will | Healthcare POA |
|---------|---|---|
| **What it is** | Document stating wishes | Document naming decision-maker |
| **Applies when** | Terminal illness + incapacity (specific condition) | Patient loses capacity (any reason) |
| **How specific** | Very specific (checklist of treatments) | Flexible and adaptive |
| **Who decides** | Written document (static) | Named surrogate (dynamic) |
| **Advantage** | Clear patient wishes | Can adapt to new situations |
| **Disadvantage** | Can't anticipate all scenarios | Surrogate might misinterpret wishes |
| **Legal force** | Binding | Binding (when valid) |

---

## **What Happens If Patient Has BOTH?**

**Ideal scenario:** Patient has both living will AND named POA.

**How they work together:**
- **If patient loses capacity with terminal diagnosis:** Living will guides decisions (very specific)
- **If patient loses capacity but condition isn't "terminal" by will's definition:** POA surrogate takes over (flexible)
- **If they conflict:** POA may argue "circumstances have changed; patient would have wanted different outcome than living will stated"

**Principle:** Living will is binding, but surrogate can argue for exceptions based on changed circumstances.

## **When Advance Directives Are Most Important**

### **Scenarios Where They Prevent Conflict:**

**Scenario 1: Patient becomes comatose after accident**
- Family says "Do everything!"
- Living will says "No CPR, comfort care only"
- **Resolution:** Living will is law. Follow it.

**Scenario 2: Patient with dementia declines cognitively**
- Current "self" (confused patient) says "I want to eat"
- Advance directive said "No feeding tube if I can't interact"
- **Resolution:** Advance directive reflects person's true values before disease; honor it.

**Scenario 3: Separated spouse argues with estranged adult child**
- Spouse claims "Mom would want everything"
- Adult child says "Mom wanted comfort care"
- No advance directive exists
- **Problem:** Hospital staff don't know who's right; likely legal battle ensues

---

## **The Informed REFUSAL vs. Advance Directives**

**Important distinction:**

**Informed Refusal** = competent patient refusing treatment RIGHT NOW
- "I don't want CPR if my heart stops"
- Applies immediately, while patient can communicate

**Advance Directive** = competent patient stating wishes for IF incapacity occurs
- "If I become terminal and can't decide, don't do CPR"
- Applies in the future IF certain conditions met

**Both embody autonomy, but different timing.**

## **Three Clinical Scenarios: What Should Happen**

### **Scenario A: Mrs. Chen (Our Case Study)**

**Situation:**
78-year-old with advanced lung cancer, alert and oriented. Says: "If I get to where I can't breathe and can't make decisions, I don't want a ventilator. Just comfort care."

Has not signed legal documents yet.

#### **What Should Nurse Do:**

1. **Document verbal statement:** Chart exactly what she said
   - "Patient verbalized wish: 'No ventilator if can't breathe; comfort care only'"
   - This has legal weight even without signature

2. **Do NOT disregard unsigned wishes:** Hospitals often accept verbal statements; document them

3. **Facilitate legal documentation:**
   - "Mrs. Chen, your wishes are important. Let me get social work to help you sign a living will TODAY so everyone knows"
   - "Who would you want to make decisions if you can't?"

4. **Discuss with family/POA candidates:**
   - "Have you talked to your daughter about these wishes?"
   - "Would she be willing to be your medical power of attorney?"

5. **Follow up:**
   - Ensure living will is signed
   - Ensure POA is named
   - Place in chart

#### **Why This Matters**
If she declines suddenly without signed documents, healthcare team is scrambling. If she HAS signed documents, her autonomy is protected.

---

### **Scenario B: Mr. Rodriguez (No Advance Directive)**

**Situation:**
82-year-old, unconscious after stroke. No advance directive in chart. Daughter says "Dad would never want tubes and machines."

There's no legal document naming her as POA.

#### **What Should Nurse Do:**

1. **Acknowledge the daughter's perspective:**
   - "I can see how much you love your father and want what's best for him"

2. **Explain the legal issue:**
   - "Without a document naming you as his medical power of attorney, I cannot legally defer to your wishes alone"
   - "We need to follow state law on surrogate decision-making"

3. **Identify surrogate hierarchy (varies by state):**
   - Typically: spouse → adult children → parents → siblings
   - OR first available person in that list

4. **Involve hospital resources:**
   - Ethics committee
   - Patient advocate
   - Social work
   - Physician who can discuss prognosis with family

5. **Work toward decision:**
   - "Tell me what your father valued"
   - "What do you think he would want?"
   - "Can you get that understanding from other family?"

6. **Document family discussions:**
   - What they said about patient's values
   - Who was present
   - What was decided

#### **Why This Matters**
Without legal designation, surrogate authority is unclear. Family conflicts can paralyze decisions. Ethics committee/court may need to intervene.

---

### **Scenario C: Ms. Kowalski (Living Will vs. Family Wish)**

**Situation:**
71-year-old in hospice with terminal cancer. Chart contains signed living will: "No CPR, no intubation, comfort care only." Son arrives and says: "Do everything to keep her alive. She didn't mean that."

#### **What Should Nurse Do:**

1. **Respect and assert the living will:**
   - "Your mother created a living will when she was healthy and thinking clearly"
   - "That document reflects her true wishes and values"
   - "We are legally and ethically obligated to honor it"

2. **Validate his emotion while maintaining boundary:**
   - "I know this is heartbreaking"
   - "You love your mother and want to fight for her"
   - "AND we have to respect what SHE decided"

3. **Educate about what YOU WILL do:**
   - "We won't do CPR, but we WILL keep her comfortable"
   - "We WILL manage her pain"
   - "We WILL support her dignity"
   - "She won't be alone"

4. **Reframe the goals:**
   - "Our focus is on quality of life, not quantity"
   - "Making sure her last days are peaceful and surrounded by family"
   - "Honoring her wishes is how we show respect"

5. **Offer concrete support:**
   - "Would you like to hold her hand?"
   - "Would you like to tell her you're here?"
   - "Can I get you anything?"

6. **Involve the treating physician:**
   - Doctor should speak to son directly
   - Discuss prognosis, explain why CPR wouldn't work (often doesn't in this context)
   - Reaffirm commitment to comfort

7. **Consider ethics committee:**
   - If family continues to insist on CPR despite living will
   - If there's concern about authenticity of living will
   - To surface family conflict and seek resolution

8. **Document everything:**
   - Family's objections
   - Your education attempts
   - Explanation of living will
   - Provider discussions

#### **Why This Matters**
Family grief can lead to requests that violate the patient's autonomy. Standing firm on advance directives (with compassion) honors patient while supporting grieving family.

## **When Advance Directives Are IGNORED or INVALID**

### **Reasons a Living Will Might Be Invalid**
- Patient was coerced to sign
- Patient didn't understand what they were signing
- Patient wasn't competent when they signed
- Signature obtained fraudulently
- State-specific requirements not met (not witnessed, not notarized)

**Nurse's responsibility:** If you suspect invalid directive, escalate to ethics committee/administration.

### **When Physicians Override Living Wills** (Very Rare)
- Patient is not actually terminal (directive applies to terminal patients)
- Physician has legal reason to question validity
- Emergency situation where directives haven't been verified yet

**Nurse's role:** Advocate for patient's wishes; question overrides.

---

## **The Anticipatory Grief Connection**

Patients who create advance directives have often done their grief work:
- Accepted mortality
- Clarified values
- Thought about what matters most
- Made peace with limitations

Families who resist advance directives often:
- Haven't done anticipatory grief work
- Are in denial about disease progression
- Feel guilt or helplessness
- Fear they're "giving up"

**Nursing implication:** Educate families gently. Help them see advance directives as *expressions of values*, not *giving up*.

## **HESI Exam Focus: Advance Directives**

HESI tests whether you understand:

1. **Types of directives** (living will vs. POA) and how they differ
2. **When they apply** (living will = terminal; POA = any incapacity)
3. **Which takes priority** (patient's documented wishes > family assumptions)
4. **Your role** (advocate for patient's wishes, support grieving family)
5. **Legal standing** (family cannot override valid directive)
6. **Therapeutic communication** (validate emotion while maintaining boundaries)

---

## **Key Points to Remember**

- Advance directive = autonomy in action
- Living will is specific but rigid
- Healthcare POA is flexible but requires trustworthy surrogate
- Family's wishes do NOT override patient's documented wishes
- Grief + education = better family cooperation
- Document everything—your education, discussions, family's understanding
- When unclear, elevate to ethics committee
