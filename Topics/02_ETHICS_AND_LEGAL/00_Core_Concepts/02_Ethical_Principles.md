# Ethics and Legal Topic 2: Ethical Principles in Nursing

## The Six Core Ethical Principles

All nursing ethics rest on six foundational principles. Understanding these helps you navigate complex situations where right answers aren't obvious.

---

## **1. AUTONOMY: Respect for Persons**

### **Definition**
The right to self-determination; respect for people's choices about their own bodies, treatment, and care.

### **What It Means in Practice**
- Patients make their own decisions (when competent)
- Informed consent is required before treatment
- Surrogates decide for incompetent patients
- Patients can refuse treatment (even life-saving treatment)
- Patients' values and preferences guide their care

### **Autonomy in Action**
✓ **Examples:**
- Letting patient refuse medication even though you think they need it
- Obtaining informed consent before procedures
- Asking patient what matters most to them
- Respecting advance directives
- Allowing patient to choose pain management method

✗ **Violations of Autonomy:**
- Forcing treatment without consent (battery)
- Withholding information needed to make decisions
- Pressuring patient toward YOUR preferred option
- Ignoring advance directives
- Making decisions for competent patients

### **Common Conflict**
Autonomy often conflicts with **beneficence** (doing good). The ethical hierarchy in modern Western nursing says: **Autonomy usually wins.**

**Example:** Patient refuses needed chemotherapy. You believe chemo will save her life. But HER autonomy to refuse takes priority (even though you want to benefit her).

### **HESI Testing**
Questions often ask: "Which action respects patient autonomy?" 
- Answer: Usually involves patient making the decision, even if you disagree

---

## **2. BENEFICENCE: Do Good**

### **Definition**
An obligation to act in the patient's best interests; to provide benefit and promote well-being.

### **What It Means in Practice**
- Provide treatments that help
- Advocate for patient improvement
- Use clinical judgment to determine what benefits patient
- Promote health and healing
- Prevent harm proactively

### **Beneficence in Action**
✓ **Examples:**
- Educating patient about treatment options
- Encouraging patient to take beneficial medication
- Providing comfort measures
- Suggesting therapies that improve outcomes
- Initiating health promotion activities

✗ **Failures of Beneficence:**
- Ignoring patient's pain ("They'll just complain more")
- Not educating about prevention
- Withholding information that would help
- Not trying alternatives when one approach isn't working
- Accepting poor outcomes when intervention could help

### **When Beneficence Conflicts with Autonomy**
This is the classic ethical tension in healthcare:
- Your clinical judgment says: "This patient needs this treatment"
- Patient's choice is: "I don't want it"
- **Modern ethics says:** Respect autonomy, BUT robustly educate about benefits

**Example:** Diabetic patient refuses insulin. Beneficence says insulin prevents complications. Autonomy says patient can refuse. Solution: Explain benefits thoroughly (beneficence), then respect refusal (autonomy).

### **HESI Testing**
Questions test whether you understand you can't FORCE beneficence on unwilling patients.

---

## **3. NON-MALEFICENCE: Do No Harm**

### **Definition**
An obligation to avoid causing harm; to minimize potential for injury or suffering.

### **What It Means in Practice**
- Prevent complications
- Use safest techniques and medications
- Assess for risks before procedures
- Monitor for adverse effects
- Advocate against harmful treatments
- Use infection control rigorously
- Proper medication dosing (don't overdose)

### **Non-Maleficence in Action**
✓ **Examples:**
- Using sterile technique to prevent infection
- Monitoring for medication side effects
- Assessing pain and preventing suffering
- Following safety protocols
- Checking patient allergies before medication
- Using proper body mechanics to prevent injury

✗ **Violations of Non-Maleficence:**
- Negligence (not using safety practices)
- Medication errors
- Contaminating sterile fields
- Ignoring known risks
- Rough handling causing injury
- Failing to monitor for complications

### **Harm Can Be Intentional or Unintentional**
- **Intentional harm:** Abuse, assault (rare, criminal)
- **Unintentional harm:** Negligence, lack of vigilance (more common)

**Both violate non-maleficence, but negligence is what licensing boards focus on.**

### **First, Do No Harm**
This is the Hippocratic principle. In practice:
- If intervention causes more harm than benefit → don't do it
- Example: Side effects of chemo outweigh benefits? Non-maleficence might argue against it
- But it's not the ONLY principle—beneficence and autonomy matter too

### **HESI Testing**
Questions about safety, infection control, medication errors, and adverse effects are testing non-maleficence.

---

## **4. JUSTICE: Fair Treatment**

### **Definition**
Fair, equitable, and impartial treatment. Treating equals equally; distributing resources fairly.

### **What It Means in Practice**
- All patients deserve same quality care regardless of ability to pay
- Don't discriminate based on race, religion, gender, sexuality
- Allocate limited resources fairly (if only 5 ICU beds, who gets them?)
- Advocate for patients with fewer resources
- Address health inequities in care
- Don't show favoritism

### **Justice in Action**
✓ **Examples:**
- Providing same pain management to all patients regardless of background
- Advocating for uninsured patient to get treatment they need
- Not giving better pain control to wealthy patients
- Ensuring patient with dementia gets same dignity as alert patient
- Fairly allocating nurse time among all patients (not neglecting difficult patients)

✗ **Violations of Justice:**
- Racial discrimination in care ("We treat this patient differently because...")
- Refusing care to uninsured patients
- Providing less attention to patients considered "difficult"
- Over-allocating resources to some patients while others go without
- Language barriers preventing equitable communication

### **The Challenge: Resource Scarcity**
Justice becomes hardest when resources ARE limited:
- Only one ICU bed left; two critical patients need it
- Not enough vaccines for everyone; who gets priority?
- Limited nursing staff; how to allocate fairly?

**Justice requires:** Transparent criteria, not personal bias

### **HESI Testing**
Questions about discrimination, equitable treatment, health disparities, resource allocation test justice.

---

## **5. FIDELITY: Loyalty & Trust**

### **Definition**
Being faithful, loyal, and trustworthy; keeping promises; maintaining confidentiality; being reliable.

### **What It Means in Practice**
- Keep patient's secrets (confidentiality)
- Do what you promise ("I'll be back in 10 minutes")
- Be consistent and reliable
- Don't abandon patients
- Speak truthfully
- Report honestly even when inconvenient
- Maintain professional relationships

### **Fidelity in Action**
✓ **Examples:**
- Keeping patient information private
- Following through on commitments
- Being present and attentive to patients
- Honest reporting on charts
- Maintaining boundaries (not gossiping about patients)
- Advocating for patient even when unpopular

✗ **Violations of Fidelity:**
- Breaking confidentiality
- Promising something then not following up
- Ignoring patient's call light
- Lying on charts
- Gossiping about patients
- Abandoning difficult patients
- Favoring some patients over others

### **Fidelity Underlies Trust**
Patients must trust nurses. Trust is built through:
- Keeping their secrets
- Following through on promises
- Being honest
- Being reliably present

**Without fidelity, the therapeutic relationship collapses.**

### **HESI Testing**
Questions about confidentiality (HIPAA), honesty in documentation, therapeutic relationships test fidelity.

---

## **6. VERACITY: Truthfulness**

### **Definition**
An obligation to be honest; to tell the truth and not deceive.

### **What It Means in Practice**
- Tell patient truth about diagnosis and treatment
- Don't hide bad news
- Be honest about limitations or uncertainties
- Don't mislead for "patient's own good"
- Report accurately what you observed
- Admit mistakes

### **Veracity in Action**
✓ **Examples:**
- Telling patient "We're still figuring out what's causing this" vs. pretending certainty
- Reporting medication error honestly
- Admitting "I don't know but I'll find out"
- Telling patient procedure will be uncomfortable (not "it won't hurt you")
- Documenting exactly what happened

✗ **Violations of Veracity:**
- Telling patient false reassurances ("Everything will be fine")
- Hiding diagnosis to "protect" them
- Lying on documentation
- Pretending medication has no side effects
- Not reporting errors
- Misleading patients about competence

### **Hard Truth: Veracity Can Hurt Patients Emotionally**
**Conflict:** Being truthful might cause emotional harm (anxiety, depression about bad diagnosis).

**However:** Lying violates autonomy (patient can't make informed decisions without truth) and fidelity (erodes trust).

**Resolution:** Be truthful, but with compassion and support. Don't just dump bad news—deliver it with care.

## **When Principles Conflict: The Ethical Hierarchy**

In modern Western nursing ethics, when principles conflict, the hierarchy is generally:

```
AUTONOMY (top - usually wins)
    ↓
JUSTICE (fair treatment)
    ↓
BENEFICENCE (do good)
VERACITY (tell truth)
FIDELITY (be trustworthy)
    ↓
NON-MALEFICENCE (do no harm - but rarely overrides autonomy)
```

**This means:**
- **Autonomy wins over beneficence:** Let competent patient refuse even if you think it's harmful
- **Justice overrides favoritism:** Don't give better care to rich patients even if you want to help them more
- **Beneficence usually outweighs non-maleficence:** If medication has side effects but helps overall, give it (if patient consents)

**HOWEVER:** This hierarchy is not absolute. Context matters, and you may need an ethics committee to sort it out.

---

## **Practical Framework: When You Face an Ethical Dilemma**

### **Step 1: Identify Which Principles Apply**
Is this about autonomy? Justice? Beneficence? Usually multiple principles are involved.

### **Step 2: Note Where They Conflict**
Does respecting autonomy mean withholding beneficial treatment? Does justice and beneficence point different directions?

### **Step 3: Apply Hierarchy**
Generally, autonomy wins. But check if justice or other factors change the picture.

### **Step 4: Escalate if Unclear**
- Talk to supervisor
- Consult ethics committee
- Call patient advocate
- Get family/proxy input

### **Step 5: Document**
Record your ethical reasoning and who you consulted.

---

## **HESI Exam Focus**

HESI questions test whether you:
1. **Can identify which principle applies** to a scenario
2. **Know when principles conflict** and which takes priority
3. **Understand autonomy usually wins** (this is heavily tested)
4. **Can apply principles to patient care** decisions

**Common question format:**
"A competent 40-year-old patient refuses chemotherapy despite advanced cancer. Which ethical principle BEST supports the nurse's action to respect this refusal?"
- A) Beneficence
- B) **Autonomy** ← CORRECT
- C) Justice
- D) Non-maleficence
