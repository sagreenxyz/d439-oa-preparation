# Elimination Topic 3: Urinary Catheterization - Types, Indications, and Infection Risk

## Overview

Urinary catheterization is one of the most common invasive procedures in healthcare. However, it's the **#1 cause of hospital-acquired urinary tract infections (CAUTIs)**. Understanding when to use catheters, which type, and how to prevent complications is critical for the HESI exam.

---

## Types of Urinary Catheters

### 1. Straight Catheter (Intermittent Catheterization)

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Structure** | Single lumen; hollow tube with opening at tip |
| **Balloon** | NO balloon—catheter is removed after single use |
| **Duration** | Temporary; used for one catheterization |
| **Size** | Typically 12-16 French (Fr) |

**Indications:**
- Obtaining a sterile urine specimen (when client cannot void voluntarily)
- Relieving acute urinary retention (single void)
- Measuring post-void residual (PVR) bladder scan
- Clients who prefer intermittent cath over indwelling

**Advantages:**
- Lower infection risk than indwelling (shorter dwell time)
- No ongoing management needed
- Less cost
- Preserves normal voiding when possible

**Disadvantages:**
- More frequent procedures if needed regularly (repeated trauma risk)
- Requires client cooperation

**Special Consideration:** Some clients with neurogenic bladder (spinal cord injury, MS) perform **clean intermittent self-catheterization (CISC)** multiple times daily. This is actually safer long-term than indwelling.

---

### 2. Indwelling Urinary Catheter (Foley Catheter)

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Structure** | Double or triple lumen catheter |
| **Lumen 1** | Urine drainage (largest bore) |
| **Lumen 2** | Balloon inflation (small port for sterile water) |
| **Lumen 3** | Irrigation port (optional, triple-lumen) |
| **Balloon** | Inflated with 10 mL sterile water to keep catheter in place |
| **Duration** | Long-term; stays in bladder continuously |
| **Size** | Typically 16-18 French (Fr) for adults |

**Indications:**
- Continuous bladder drainage (cannot void voluntarily)
- Monitoring accurate urine output (ICU, critical illness)
- Severe incontinence (skin integrity at risk, palliative care)
- Acute urinary retention with obstruction
- Perioperative and major surgery (especially pelvic or prolonged)
- Spinal cord injury with neurogenic bladder (some clients)
- End-of-life/palliative care (comfort)
- Urethral/bladder trauma requiring healing

**Advantages:**
- Continuous drainage without repeated procedures
- Reduced trauma from repeated catheterizations
- Accurate intake/output measurement
- Comfort in certain situations (end-of-life)

**Disadvantages:**
- **HIGH infection risk** (foreign body, biofilm formation)
- Requires ongoing catheter care and maintenance
- Risk of obstruction, encrustation, blockage
- Urethral trauma with long-term use
- Discomfort if client mobile
- Psychological impact (dignity concerns)

---

### 3. Suprapubic Catheter

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Placement** | Inserted directly into bladder through abdominal wall (suprapubic region) |
| **Advantage** | Bypasses urethra; reduces urethral trauma and infection risk |
| **Use** | Long-term drainage in those with urethral obstruction, severe trauma, or neurogenic bladder |
| **Special consideration** | Requires sterile insertion procedure; ongoing site care needed |

---

### 4. Condom (External) Catheter

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Structure** | External sheath applied over penis; drains to collection bag |
| **Advantage** | Non-invasive; lower infection risk than indwelling |
| **Use** | Incontinent males when skin integrity at risk; intermittent drainage acceptable |
| **Disadvantage** | Does not work for females; requires intact skin; frequent changes needed |
| **Complication** | Skin breakdown if not changed frequently or if applied too tightly |

## **CRITICAL: Why Catheterization = High Infection Risk**

### Mechanism of CAUTI (Catheter-Associated UTI)

1. **Urethral trauma:** Catheter insertion damages epithelial lining → breaks protective barrier
2. **Bacteria introduction:** Potential for introducing organisms during insertion (hence STERILE technique critical)
3. **Loss of normal flushing:** Bypassing normal urethra → no cleansing urine flow to wash out organisms
4. **Biofilm formation:** Catheter provides a surface for bacteria to colonize and form **biofilm**—a protective matrix that antibiotics cannot penetrate
5. **Prolonged dwell time:** Indwelling catheters stay in longer → more time for biofilm to establish
6. **Urine stasis:** If catheter becomes blocked, urine backs up → bacterial overgrowth

### Most Common CAUTI Organisms

**Listed by frequency:**
1. **Escherichia coli (E. coli)** - Most common gram-negative rod
2. **Pseudomonas aeruginosa** - Opportunistic, antibiotic-resistant
3. **Proteus mirabilis** - Causes alkaline urine, crystal/encrustation formation
4. **Klebsiella pneumoniae** - Gram-negative rod
5. **Enterococcus** - Gram-positive
6. **Staphylococcus aureus** - (Less common in UTI; more common in bloodstream infections)

**Why E. coli leads the list:** It's part of normal gut flora; easily colonizes catheter and can quickly form biofilm.

## Prevention of CAUTIs: Key Nursing Actions

### At Insertion
- Use **strict sterile technique** (not just clean)
- Confirm indication from provider order
- Use smallest appropriate catheter size (reduces trauma)
- Use appropriate lubricant (sterile, water-soluble, may contain lidocaine for comfort)
- Ensure proper anatomic visualization during insertion

### During Maintenance
- Keep drainage bag **lower than bladder** (prevents reflux)
- Maintain **closed drainage system** (never open the system unless absolutely necessary)
- Keep tubing **free of kinks and obstruction**
- Ensure **catheter is taped securely** (prevents movement and traction)
  - **Female:** Tape to inner thigh
  - **Male:** Tape to abdomen or top of thigh (leaving angle for penis)
- Perform **daily perineal care** (not aggressive scrubbing; gentle with cleansing agent)
- Monitor for signs of **catheter obstruction** (no output, reduced output, suprapubic pain)

### Duration Management
- **Remove as soon as clinically appropriate** (don't leave in "just in case")
- Reassess need **daily**
- For long-term catheterization: **scheduled catheter changes** per protocol (usually every 4-6 weeks) or PRN if blocked
- AVOID **routine catheter irrigation** unless clinically indicated

### Assessment of Catheter Complications
- **No output for >6 hours:** Assess catheter for kinks, blockage; check for bladder distension; may need irrigation or catheter change
- **Output is dark, concentrated, or cloudy:** May indicate dehydration or infection
- **Suprapubic pain/distension:** Catheter may be blocked
- **Fever with catheter in place:** Assume CAUTI until proven otherwise
- **Hematuria:** May be normal after insertion for 24-48 hours; persistent bleeding requires assessment

## Straight Catheter vs. Indwelling Catheter: Quick Comparison

| Aspect | **Straight Catheter** | **Indwelling (Foley)** |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Lumen** | Single | Double/Triple |
| **Balloon** | No | Yes (inflated 10 mL) |
| **Duration** | Single use (minutes) | Continuous (days to weeks) |
| **Insertion** | At bedside | Usually at bedside (some in OR) |
| **Infection risk** | **LOWER** (single procedure) | **HIGHER** (prolonged use) |
| **Tech used** | Straight catheterization | Indwelling catheterization |
| **When to use** | Specimen collection, relief of single retention episode | Continuous drainage, accurate I&O, palliative |
| **Trauma** | Single episode of trauma | Ongoing irritation potential |
| **Cost** | Lower (one catheter, one procedure) | Higher (catheter, supplies, labor for maintenance) |

## HESI Exam Focus

1. **Know the indications:** You must be able to justify why an indwelling catheter is appropriate
2. **Recognize CAUTI risk:** Questions will ask about prevention strategies
3. **Straight vs. indwelling:** Understand when each is appropriate
4. **Infection organisms:** E. coli is #1; know the mechanism
5. **Red flags:** Blocked catheter, fever with catheter, no output = escalation
6. **Prevention strategies:** Sterile technique, closed system, timely removal
7. **Biofilm concept:** Explains why long-term catheters get recurrent infections and why removal is best prevention

---

## Critical Learning Points for Catheterization as a Whole

- **Catheterization is high-risk:** The most common cause of hospital-acquired UTI
- **Prevention is the goal:** Best strategy is to remove catheters as soon as possible
- **Strict sterile technique is non-negotiable:** Insertion contamination = immediate CAUTI risk
- **Closed drainage system must be maintained:** Opening it introduces bacteria
- **Daily assessment is essential:** Monitor for obstruction, signs of infection, need for continued catheterization
