# Elimination Topic 1: Normal Urine Output & Assessment

## Standard Output Benchmarks

### Daily Urine Output
- **Normal 24-hour output:** 720 mL to 2400 mL (varies by individual hydration status)
- **Standard expected output:** ~30 mL/hour
- **Per 8-hour shift:** 240 mL minimum
- **Abnormality threshold:** <30 mL/hour = oliguria

### Critical Findings Requiring Escalation
- **Oliguria:** <30 mL/hour (indicates possible acute kidney injury, dehydration, or shock)
- **Anuria:** Little to no urine output (medical emergency)

---

## Factors Affecting Urine Output

### Factors That INCREASE Output
- Diuretic medications (loop, thiazide, osmotic diuretics)
- Increased fluid intake (oral or IV)
- Hyperglycemia (osmotic diuresis in uncontrolled diabetes)
- Reduced ADH (diabetes insipidus)
- Caffeine
- Alcohol

### Factors That DECREASE Output
- **Medications:** ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs, beta-blockers
- **Conditions causing fluid loss:** Sepsis, burns, diarrhea, vomiting
- **Conditions affecting kidney perfusion:** Hypotension, shock, congestive heart failure
- **Renal pathology:** Acute kidney injury (AKI), chronic kidney disease
- **Dehydration**
- **Urinary obstruction:** Blocked catheter, stones, BPH in males
- **ADH excess:** SIADH (syndrome of inappropriate ADH)

---

## Mechanism: Medication Effects on GFR

### ACE Inhibitors
- **Normal function:** Angiotensin II causes vasoconstriction of the *efferent arteriole* (vessel leaving the glomerulus)
- **Mechanism of decreased GFR:** ACE inhibitors block angiotensin II formation → loss of efferent vasoconstriction → decreased glomerular pressure → decreased GFR
- **Clinical risk:** Acute kidney injury in dehydrated, elderly, or CKD patients
- **HESI Pearl:** If you see a dehydrated client on ACE inhibitors, expect answers about holding the medication and rehydrating

### NSAIDs (Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs)
- **Normal function:** Prostaglandins dilate the *afferent arteriole* (vessel entering the glomerulus)
- **Mechanism of decreased GFR:** NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandins → loss of afferent dilation → decreased glomerular blood flow → decreased GFR
- **Additional risk:** Direct renal tubular damage with chronic use
- **Clinical risk:** Particularly dangerous in dehydrated clients, those with renal disease, or elderly

---

## Assessment Questions to Ask

**For clients with urine output abnormalities:**
- How much fluid have you been drinking in the last 24 hours?
- Any recent diuretic or NSAID use?
- Changes in symptoms (weight gain, edema, shortness of breath)?
- Fever or signs of infection?
- Current medications?
- History of kidney disease?

---

## HESI Exam Focus Areas

1. **Know the numbers:** 30 mL/hr, 240 mL/shift, 720 mL/day
2. **Recognize oliguria as urgent:** Questions will ask what this signals
3. **Drug mechanisms:** ACE inhibitors and NSAIDs are frequently tested
4. **Dehydration + medications = kidney risk:** High-yield combination
5. **When to escalate:** Oliguria/anuria require provider notification
