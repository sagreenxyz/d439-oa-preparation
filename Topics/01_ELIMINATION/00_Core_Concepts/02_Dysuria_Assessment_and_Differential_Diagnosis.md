# Elimination Topic 2: Dysuria Assessment & Differential Diagnosis

## What is Dysuria?
**Dysuria** = difficult, painful, or burning urination. It's a symptom, not a diagnosis—so the nursing priority is identifying the underlying cause.

---

## Assessment Framework: PQRST for Dysuria Pain

When a client reports dysuria, use the systematic pain assessment:

- **P - Provocation/Palliation**
  - What makes it worse? (urination, position, intercourse, certain foods/drinks)
  - What makes it better? (medications, rest, warm compress, voiding)

- **Q - Quality/Quantity**
  - Describe the sensation: burning, stinging, sharp, dull, aching?
  - Severity on 0-10 scale

- **R - Region/Radiation**
  - Localized to urethra? Entire bladder region? Perineal area? Flank?
  - Does pain radiate anywhere?

- **S - Severity**
  - Intensity (mild, moderate, severe)
  - Impact on daily activities

- **T - Timing**
  - When did it start? (sudden vs. gradual)
  - Constant or intermittent?
  - Pattern (worse at certain times?)

## Differential Diagnosis: Dysuria by Cause

### 1. Acute Cystitis (UTI - Bladder Infection)

| Finding | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Pain location** | Suprapubic (lower abdomen), throughout bladder region |
| **Pain timing** | Dysuria throughout voiding (beginning, middle, and end) |
| **Associated symptoms** | Urgency, frequency (polyuria), hematuria (may be visible), fever, chills if severe |
| **Diagnostic clue** | Urinalysis shows WBCs, bacteria, nitrites; urine culture positive for bacteria |
| **Distinguishing feature** | **Systemic symptoms** (fever) + internal urinary symptoms |

**Why it happens:** Bacteria (usually gram-negative) ascend from urethra to bladder, causing inflammation.

---

### 2. Urethritis (Urethral Inflammation)

| Finding | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Pain location** | Urethra (anterior, often felt at meatus) |
| **Pain timing** | Dysuria at **BEGINNING of voiding** especially; may diminish as bladder empties |
| **Associated symptoms** | Urethral discharge (white, yellow, green, clear), minimal urgency/frequency |
| **Diagnostic clue** | Urinalysis may show WBCs but fewer bacteria than cystitis; culture negative |
| **Distinguishing feature** | **Discharge present**, pain at start of void only |

**Why it happens:** Urethral trauma (catheterization, sexual activity), STI (gonorrhea, chlamydia), non-infectious inflammation.

---

### 3. Candida Vulvovaginitis (Yeast Infection)

| Finding | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Pain location** | **EXTERNAL** (vulva, vagina) NOT internal urethra |
| **Pain type** | Itching, burning at external genitalia, NOT internal urinary pain |
| **Associated symptoms** | Thick, white, cottage-cheese-like discharged; vulvar erythema, edema |
| **Diagnostic clue** | Urinalysis normal (no bacteria, no WBCs); vaginal wet mount shows yeast |
| **Distinguishing feature** | **Discharge appearance** (thick, white, chunky); external symptoms only |

**Why it happens:** Overgrowth of *Candida albicans*. Risk factors: antibiotics, diabetes, immunosuppression, pregnancy, tight clothing.

**CRITICAL HESI POINT:** Candida causes *EXTERNAL* dysuria (vulvar pain), not true urinary dysuria. Many students confuse this!

---

### 4. Bacterial Vaginosis (BV)

| Finding | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Pain location** | Vaginal area (external) |
| **Pain type** | Itching, irritation (less severe burning than candida) |
| **Associated symptoms** | Thin, gray-white discharge with **FISHY ODOR** (especially after intercourse), vaginal irritation |
| **Diagnostic clue** | Whiff test positive (fishy smell when KOH added to discharge); wet mount shows "clue cells" |
| **Distinguishing feature** | **Fishy odor** is the hallmark finding |

**Why it happens:** Disruption of normal vaginal flora; overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria.

---

### 5. Urinary Stones (Calculi)

| Finding | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Pain location** | **FLANK pain** (side, below ribs) if kidney/ureter; **SUPRAPUBIC** if bladder stone |
| **Pain type** | **COLICKY** pain—intermittent, severe, waves of intensity |
| **Associated symptoms** | **Gross hematuria** (visible blood), nausea/vomiting, restlessness, inability to find comfortable position |
| **Diagnostic clue** | Stone visible on imaging (CT, ultrasound); hematuria on urinalysis |
| **Distinguishing feature** | Severe colicky flank pain + gross hematuria = high suspicion for stone |

**Why it happens:** Precipitation of minerals (calcium oxalate, uric acid, phosphate) in urinary system.

**HESI PEARL:** Kidney stone pain is **SEVERE and colicky** (comes in waves). Client is often writhing, unable to stay still. This is DIFFERENT from UTI pain which is more burning/constant.

---

### 6. Prostatitis (Males Only)

| Finding | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Pain location** | Perineal (between scrotum and anus), lower back, lower abdomen, may have testicular pain |
| **Pain type** | Dull, aching, sharp—may be constant or intermittent |
| **Associated symptoms** | Dysuria, urinary frequency/urgency, low-grade fever, malaise, possible urgency with retention, discharge |
| **Diagnostic clue** | Digital rectal exam (DRE) shows tender/enlarged prostate; urinalysis shows WBCs and bacteria; culture positive |
| **Distinguishing feature** | **Perineal/testicular pain** in a male + dysuria = prostatitis until proven otherwise |

**Types:**
- **Acute bacterial:** High fever, severe dysuria, possible retention
- **Chronic bacterial:** Recurrent UTIs in male (unusual—suggests prostatitis)
- **Chronic pelvic pain syndrome:** No bacteria cultured, but symptoms persist

---

### 7. Other Causes of Dysuria

**Chemical irritation:**
- Douches, perfumed products, spermicides, tight clothing
- Presentation: External dysuria, itching, no infection on UA

**Urethral trauma:**
- From catheterization, rough sexual activity, straddle injury
- Presentation: Dysuria with history of recent procedure/trauma

**Radiation cystitis:**
- From pelvic radiation therapy
- Presentation: Chronic dysuria, hematuria, urgency

## Quick Differential Summary Table

| Condition | **Pain Location** | **PAY ATTENTION** | **Associated Finding** |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------|----------------------|
| **Cystitis (UTI)** | Suprapubic/bladder | Fever, urgency, frequency, hematuria | Pyuria on UA |
| **Urethritis** | Urethra (anterior) | Discharge, pain at start of void | Urethral drainage |
| **Candida** | **EXTERNAL** vulva | Thick white discharge, itching | Normal urinalysis |
| **BV** | **EXTERNAL** vagina | **FISHY ODOR** | Clue cells on wet mount |
| **Stones** | **FLANK (colicky)** | **SEVERE**, writhing, gross hematuria | Stone on imaging |
| **Prostatitis** | **PERINEAL** (male) | Fever, retention risk, tender DRE | Enlarged prostate |

## HESI Exam Strategy

1. **Listen to the location:** External symptoms = vaginal/vulvar; internal = urinary tract
2. **Look for fever:** Fever + dysuria = bacterial infection (UTI or prostatitis), not just irritation
3. **Hematuria is key:** Gross hematuria + colicky flank pain = strongly suggests stones, NOT UTI
4. **Discharge character:** Thick white = candida; fishy-smelling = BV; purulent = urethritis/UTI
5. **Pain pattern:** Colicky (waves) = stones; constant burning = UTI; external itch = yeast/BV
6. **Male client with dysuria:** Always consider prostatitis as primary diagnosis

---

## Red Flags Requiring Immediate Escalation

- Colicky flank pain with hematuria (stone with possible obstruction)
- Fever + dysuria + unable to void (possible retention with infection)
- Gross hematuria without pain (possible malignancy or severe infection—requires workup)
- Prostatitis symptoms in male (risk of sepsis if untreated)
