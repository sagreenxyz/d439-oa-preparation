## Topic Overview

**Elimination** is the first foundational topic covering urinary system assessment, physiology, and procedural nursing care. This topic is critical because:

1. **Elimination is a Maslow basic need**—Foundations courses build on hierarchy of basic human needs
2. **Catheterization is the #1 cause of hospital-acquired infections (HAIs)**—safety critical
3. **Assessment skills are foundational**—dysuria, retention, output abnormalities are common clinical presentations
4. **Procedural competency required**—sterile technique for catheterization is foundational clinical skill
5. **HESI heavily tests this area**—expect multiple questions on catheterization indications, complications, and prevention

## Content Domains Covered

### Domain 1: Urinary Output Assessment & Monitoring
**Learning Objectives:**
- ✅ Identify normal urine output parameters (30 mL/hr, 720 mL/24h, 240 mL/8h)
- ✅ Differentiate oliguria and anuria; recognize as urgent findings
- ✅ Identify factors affecting output (medications, dehydration, kidney perfusion)
- ✅ Understand drug mechanisms (ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs) affecting GFR

**Key Mechanisms:**
- **ACE Inhibitors:** Block angiotensin II → lose efferent vasoconstriction → ↓GFR
- **NSAIDs:** Inhibit prostaglandins → lose afferent dilation → ↓GFR

**HESI Focus:**
- Know the numbers cold (30 mL/hr is the threshold)
- Recognize dehydration + ACE/NSAID = very high AKI risk
- Understand when oliguria requires escalation

### Domain 2: Dysuria Assessment & Differential Diagnosis
**Learning Objectives:**
- ✅ Use PQRST pain framework (Provocation, Quality, Region, Severity, Timing)
- ✅ Differentiate 9+ causes of dysuria
- ✅ Distinguish internal (urinary) vs. external (vaginal/vulvar) dysuria
- ✅ Identify key differentiators: pain location, discharge character, fever, UA findings

**9 Dysuria Causes Covered:**
1. Acute cystitis (UTI)
2. Urethritis
3. Candida vulvovaginitis
4. Bacterial vaginosis
5. Urinary stones
6. Prostatitis
7. STIs (gonorrhea, chlamydia)
8. Chemical/irritant causes
9. Herpes simplex virus (HSV)

**Key Differentiators:**
- **Location:** UTI = internal suprapubic; vaginitis = external; stones = flank
- **Fever:** Present = infection; absent = irritation
- **Discharge:** Thick white (candida); fishy (BV); purulent (urethritis); none (UTI/stones)
- **Pain pattern:** Colicky/waves (stones); burning/constant (UTI); itching/irritation (vaginitis)

**HESI Focus:**
- Questions will present vignettes; you must identify diagnosis
- Distractors will be plausible but wrong—know differentiators
- "External dysuria" is THE key differentiator for candida/BV

### Domain 3: Urinary Catheterization—Types & Indications
**Learning Objectives:**
- ✅ Understand catheter types (straight, indwelling, suprapubic, external)
- ✅ Identify indications for catheterization (7+ scenarios)
- ✅ Know why catheterization has high infection risk
- ✅ Understand biofilm formation and its clinical implications

**Catheter Comparison:**

| Type | Duration | Use | Lumen | Infection Risk |
|------|----------|-----|-------|---|
| **Straight** | Single use (minutes) | Specimen, single void | Single | LOW |
| **Indwelling (Foley)** | Continuous (days-weeks) | Continuous drainage | Double/Triple | HIGH |
| **Suprapubic** | Long-term | Bypass urethra | Single | MODERATE |
| **External (Condom)** | Continuous | Males, incontinent | N/A | LOW |

**Indications for Indwelling Catheterization (7+ scenarios):**
1. **Inability to void:** Neurogenic bladder, acute retention, severe cognitive impairment
2. **I&O monitoring:** ICU, hemodynamic instability, sepsis assessment
3. **Skin protection:** Severe incontinence with breakdown risk
4. **Palliative care:** End-of-life comfort and dignity
5. **Perioperative:** Major abdominal/pelvic surgery
6. **Trauma:** Urethral/bladder injury requiring healing
7. **Obstruction:** BPH, stones, strictures causing retention

**What NOT to use catheters for:**
- ❌ Convenience (use other alternatives)
- ❌ Incontinence alone (use external cath or briefs)
- ❌ Specimen collection (use straight cath)

**HESI Focus:**
- Know the comprehensive indications—HESI will ask about multiple scenarios
- Match clinical presentation to indication
- Watch for distractors offering convenience or routine incontinence as justification

### Domain 4: CAUTI Prevention—Mechanism & Strategy
**Learning Objectives:**
- ✅ Understand biofilm formation and its role in CAUTIs
- ✅ Identify common CAUTI organisms (E. coli #1)
- ✅ Know prevention strategies (sterile insertion, closed system, secured catheter, early removal)
- ✅ Recognize signs of CAUTI and complications

**CAUTI Development Process:**
1. **Insertion:** Epithelial trauma if not sterile technique
2. **Colonization (hours):** Bacteria adhere to catheter surface
3. **Biofilm formation (24-48h):** Bacteria secrete protective matrix
4. **Established CAUTI (days):** Dense biofilm resistant to antibiotics
5. **Complications:** Obstruction, urosepsis, kidney damage

**Biofilm Concept (CRITICAL):**
- Biofilm is a protective bacterial community inside extracellular matrix
- Antibiotics cannot penetrate biofilm matrix
- Bacteria inside biofilm are 100-1000x more resistant than free-floating bacteria
- **Best treatment = catheter REMOVAL, not antibiotics**
- **Best prevention = minimize dwell time**

**Common CAUTI Organisms:**
1. E. coli (most common)
2. Pseudomonas aeruginosa
3. Proteus mirabilis
4. Klebsiella pneumoniae
5. Enterococcus

**Prevention Strategies:**
- Sterile insertion technique (prevents introduction)
- Smallest appropriate catheter size (reduces trauma)
- Secure catheter taping (prevents movement/microtrauma)
- Keep system closed (prevents repeated inoculation)
- Keep tubing unobstructed (prevents biofilm nutrient source)
- Daily perineal care (reduces bacterial load)
- **Early removal (prevents biofilm establishment)**
- Daily reassessment: "Do we still need this catheter?"

**Red Flags:**
- No output >6 hours (check obstruction)
- Fever with catheter in place (assume CAUTI)
- Suprapubic pain/distension (possible obstruction)
- Cloudy/concentrated urine (possible CAUTI)

**HESI Focus:**
- Know why biofilm forms and why it matters (explains treatment strategy)
- Recognize that asymptomatic bacteriuria in catheterized client = don't treat with antibiotics; treat with removal
- Prevention strategies are high-yield questions

## Typical HESI Questions on Elimination

### Question Type 1: Output Calculation & Interpretation
*"A client has a urine output of 100 mL in 2 hours. What does the nurse recognize?*
- A) Normal output
- B) Oliguria—requires assessment
- C) Polyuria—increase fluids
- D) Anuria—medical emergency

→ **Answer: B** (100 mL in 2h = 50 mL/h, which is >30 = not emergency, but above normal; assess)

### Question Type 2: Drug Mechanism Application
*"An elderly dehydrated client is admitted on lisinopril (ACE inhibitor) for hypertension. What is the PRIMARY concern?"*
- A) Hyperkalemia from ACE inhibitor
- B) Acute kidney injury from decreased GFR
- C) Hypotension from medication
- D) Hyperglycemia from medication

→ **Answer: B** (Dehydration + ACE inhibitor = double hit on GFR; high AKI risk)

### Question Type 3: Dysuria Differential
*"A 35-year-old female reports burning pain at the vaginal opening, has thick white discharge, and denies urinary frequency/urgency. Temperature 98.6°F. What is the likely diagnosis?"*
- A) Acute cystitis
- B) Candida vulvovaginitis
- C) Bacterial vaginosis
- D) Urethritis

→ **Answer: B** (External dysuria + thick white discharge = candida)

### Question Type 4: Catheterization Indication
*"Which client is the BEST candidate for an indwelling urinary catheter?"*
- A) 72-yr with incontinence due to weakness; skin intact
- B) 68-yr in ICU with sepsis, on vasopressors; needs accurate I&O
- C) 45-yr having simple surgery tomorrow; low infection risk
- D) 35-yr with urinary urgency without retention

→ **Answer: B** (Sepsis monitoring requires accurate output; I&O assessment is critical)

### Question Type 5: CAUTI Prevention
*"A client has had an indwelling catheter for 3 weeks. Culture shows E. coli sensitive to levofloxacin, but client is asymptomatic. What should the nurse do?"*
- A) Recommend antibiotic therapy
- B) Recommend catheter removal
- C) Monitor and reculture in 1 week
- D) Change catheter and recult

→ **Answer: B** (Asymptomatic bacteriuria in chronic catheterization = don't treat with antibiotics; treat with removal; biofilm won't respond to antibiotics)
