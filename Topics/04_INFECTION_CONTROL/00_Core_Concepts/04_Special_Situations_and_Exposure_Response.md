# Special Infection Control Situations: C. difficile, Waste, Patient Sequencing, Occupational Exposure

---

## 1. *CLOSTRIDIOIDES DIFFICILE* SPECIAL HANDLING

### Spore Resistance Challenge
*C. difficile* forms **spores** that alcohol disinfectants CANNOT kill.
- Spores: dormant, highly resistant bacterial form
- Alcohol-based sanitizers: effective against vegetative cells only
- Spore coating: impermeable to alcohol; requires mechanical removal

### Patient Education for Discharge with *C. difficile*
**ALWAYS teach: "Use soap and water, NOT hand sanitizer"**
- Explain that alcohol-based sanitizers don't work against *C. difficile* spores
- Household contacts at risk if hand hygiene inadequate
- Emphasize mechanical action of soap/water removes spores
- Environment may harbor spores; household disinfection encouraged

### Clinical Setting Hand Hygiene
- Soap and water preferred for all staff in *C. difficile* rooms
- Alcohol-based sanitizers ineffective and waste supply/time
- Standard precautions + contact precautions
- Bathroom hygiene critical (spores in feces)

### Antibiotic Management (Nursing Knowledge)
- Minimize unnecessary antibiotics (risk factor for *C. difficile* overgrowth)
- Closures case management if recurrent CDI
- Probiotics: limited evidence; not standard recommendation

## 2. WASTE DISPOSAL BY TRANSMISSION RISK

### Regular (Non-Biohazard) Trash
**Can safely discard:**
- Tissues contaminated with **respiratory secretions only** (saliva, sputum from TB, measles, influenza)
- Gloves (if not contaminated with blood)
- Paper towels from patient care areas
- Non-reusable items contaminated with respiratory secretions
- Used on contact/droplet/airborne precaution patients (IF secretion-only contact)

### Biohazard/Red Bag Waste
**MUST use red bag (biohazard container) for:**
- Items contaminated with **blood** (even small amounts)
- Sharps (needles, scalpels, broken glass)
- Pathological/microbiological waste
- Body parts/tissues
- Items contaminated with blood and body fluids mixed

### Key Distinction
- **Respiratory secretions alone ≠ biohazard waste** (saves disposal cost)
- **Blood contamination = biohazard waste** (ALWAYS, regardless of other fluids present)

### HESI Application
From airborne TB room, discarding tissues and used supplies:
- Regular trash: tissues, paper, IV tubing (no blood)
- Biohazard: sharps, any bloody materials

## 3. PATIENT VISIT SEQUENCING BY PRECAUTION LEVEL

### The Logic: Least Contaminated → Most Contaminated
Prevents bringing pathogens FROM high-precaution patients TO vulnerable unprotected patients.

### Correct Sequence
1. **Clean patient** (no precautions) → visit first
2. **Contact precautions** (skin/environmental, gloves/gown)
3. **Droplet precautions** (large respiratory droplets, mask)
4. **Airborne precautions** (TB, measles; N95 mask) → visit LAST

### Why This Order
- Start with patient needing minimal protection
- End in most restrictive precaution room
- If you acquire airborne pathogen exposure at END of assignment, no other patients are at risk
- Reverse order = bringing airborne pathogens TO unprotected clean patient

### Between-Patient Hygiene
- Hand hygiene REQUIRED between each patient
- Change gown/mask if soiled between patients
- Unit equipment may need disinfection per facility protocol

### HESI Trap
- Answer showing: "Visit airborne patient first, then clean patient" = **WRONG**
- Correct: Airborne precautions LAST

## 4. OCCUPATIONAL EXPOSURE RESPONSE

### Needlestick/Sharps Injury Immediate Response

#### Timeline: CRITICAL FOR PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis)
- **Most effective if PEP started within 2 hours**
- **Still potentially effective up to 72 hours**
- **After 72 hours: effectiveness drops significantly**

#### Correct Sequence
1. **Report to occupational health/employee health IMMEDIATELY**
   - Do NOT delay to wash wound or complete documentation
   - Do NOT wait for patient HIV test results
2. **Get baseline labs drawn and PEP evaluation initiated**
   - Blood work: Your baseline HIV, Hep B, Hep C status
   - Patient's infectious disease risk assessment
3. **Wound washing** (part of occupational health protocol, not your first action)
4. **Document incident** (important, but NOT before occupational health notification)

#### PEP Protocol
- For unknown HIV status: assume positive risk until cleared
- Antiretroviral PEP regimen: typically 3-drug combination
- Duration: 28 days
- Baseline and follow-up testing: typically at baseline, 6 weeks, 3 months, 6 months
- Adherence critical (can accept high side effects for HIV prevention)

#### Bloodborne Pathogen Testing (Patient)
- Request patient's HIV/Hep B/Hep C status
- Patient has right to refuse testing in some jurisdictions
- Staff baseline testing DONE regardless of patient's status
- PEP started based on exposure risk, not waiting for results

### Other Exposure Scenarios
- **Splash to mucous membranes (eyes, mouth):** Wash immediately, report, may require baseline labs
- **Non-intact skin exposure:** Higher risk if cuts/abrasions present; report same urgency as needlestick
- **Intact skin contact:** Lower risk; standard hand hygiene usually sufficient, but document

### Incident Report
- Document AFTER occupational health notification
- Include: device, patient source, circumstances, time of exposure, intervention taken
- Not punitive; protects employee and institution

## HESI HIGH-YIELD RULES FOR SPECIAL SITUATIONS

| Situation | Key Rule |
|-----------|----------|
| *C. difficile* patient discharge | "Use soap and water, not hand sanitizer" |
| Respiratory waste from TB room | Regular trash acceptable (no blood); no biohazard needed |
| Multiple precaution patients | Visit clean → contact → droplet → airborne (reverse of precaution intensity) |
| Needlestick injury | Report to occupational health FIRST; PEP evaluation time-critical (2-hour window) |
| Unknown HIV status exposure | Assume positive risk; initiate PEP immediately while awaiting results |
| Sharps disposal | Biohazard container ALWAYS; no exceptions |

## CLINICAL APPLICATION: INTEGRATED SCENARIO

**Patient admitted with suspected measles + respiratory secretions + needle used for labs yesterday + possible exposure from staff.**

**Your actions:**
1. ✅ Don N95 mask, gown, gloves → airborne + standard precautions
2. ✅ Implement negative-pressure room (if available)
3. ✅ Teach hand hygiene BEFORE leaving room (patient on droplet precautions to others)
4. ✅ Tissue disposal: regular trash (respiratory secretions only)
5. ✅ Sharps from lab: biohazard red bag
6. ✅ Sequence other patient visits: this patient LAST of your assignments
7. ✅ If accidental needlestick during care: occupational health FIRST, PEP evaluation immediately
