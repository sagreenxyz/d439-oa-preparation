# Standard Precautions and Hand Hygiene Principles

## Core Concept: Hand Hygiene is Context-Dependent, Not Patient-Contact Dependent

### Hand Hygiene Triggers
Hand hygiene is required:
- **Before and after room entry** (regardless of direct patient contact)
- Before donning gloves
- After removing gloves
- After touching patient/patient environment
- After touching bodily fluids, secretions, or contaminated surfaces
- Before handling clean or sterile items
- After handling patient care equipment

### Critical Distinction
- **Hand hygiene ≠ patient contact only**
- Entering a room to check IV fluids (not touching patient) still requires hand hygiene
- Hand hygiene applies to **room environment** interaction, not just direct patient care

### Hand Hygiene Methods

#### Soap and Water (Gold Standard for Certain Pathogens)
**When soap and water is REQUIRED:**
- *Clostridioides difficile* spores (alcohol does NOT kill spores)
- Norovirus
- Rotavirus
- Any visibly soiled hands

**Why:** Mechanical action of water removes spores; alcohol cannot penetrate spore coating

#### Alcohol-Based Hand Sanitizers
**Effective against:**
- Vegetative bacteria (MRSA, S. aureus)
- Most enveloped viruses
- Fungi
- **NOT effective against:** Spore-forming organisms

**Limitation:** Cannot be used if hands are visibly soiled

### HESI High-Yield Rules
1. **Enter/exit room = hand hygiene required** (before gloves donning, after glove removal)
2. **C. difficile suspected = soap and water ONLY** (no hand sanitizer)
3. **No visible soiling = alcohol-based sanitizer acceptable** (for speed/convenience)
4. **After patient care = hand hygiene before touching clean environments**

### Clinical Application
- Standard precautions apply to ALL patients regardless of diagnosis
- Hand hygiene prevents both HAI transmission and cross-contamination
- Patient doesn't need to touch you directly for hand hygiene to be necessary
- More hand hygiene ≠ harm; most common reason for HAI prevention failure
