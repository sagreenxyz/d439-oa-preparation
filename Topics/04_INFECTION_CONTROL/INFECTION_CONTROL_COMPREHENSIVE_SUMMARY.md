# Infection Control Comprehensive Summary

## Topic Overview
**Infection Control** covers the foundational knowledge and clinical decision-making required to prevent hospital-acquired infections (HAIs) and occupational exposure. HESI tests both procedural knowledge (hand hygiene, precautions, PPE) and judgment skills (when to implement precautions, waste segregation, time-critical response).

**HESI Emphasis:** Clinical judgment under pressure—not just knowing rules, but applying them efficiently.

---

## Core Content Mastered

### 1. Standard Precautions (Universal Approach)
- **Hand hygiene is context-dependent:** Entry/exit from room, not just patient contact
- **Triggers for hand hygiene:** Before/after glove donning, before clean/sterile items, after body fluid contact
- **Method selection:** Soap & water (C. difficile spores) vs. alcohol-based sanitizers (other organisms)
- **C. difficile exception:** Spores non-responsive to alcohol; must use soap and water

### 2. Transmission-Based Precautions
**Airborne (N95, negative pressure):**
- TB (pulmonary/laryngeal), measles, varicella
- Implement immediately on suspicion (don't wait for confirmation)

**Droplet (surgical mask, 3-6 feet):**
- Influenza, pertussis, meningococcal, mumps, rubella
- Larger particles; shorter transmission distance

**Contact (gloves, gown):**
- MRSA, C. difficile, VRE, norovirus
- Skin/environmental surface transmission
- Implement immediately on suspicion; not respiratory-based

### 3. Sterile Technique and Field Management
- **Outer wrapper is non-sterile by design:** Touching it doesn't contaminate contents
- **Sterile field integrity issues:** Below-waist contact, unsterile hand touch to contents, moisture seeping, uncovered field
- **Dressing change sequencing:** Set field up → hand hygiene → don sterile gloves → remove old dressing

### 4. PPE Donning and Doffing
**Donning Sequence:** Gown → mask → eye protection → gloves
**Doffing Sequence (Critical):** Gloves → gown → mask → eye protection
- Doffing sequence minimizes self-contamination by removing most contaminated layer first

### 5. Special Pathogens and Scenarios
- **MRSA:** Contact precautions; not respiratory
- **C. difficile:** Spore-resistant; soap and water for hand hygiene; contact precautions
- **Measles:** Airborne + respiratory isolation
- **TB:** Suspected = airborne precautions immediately (don't wait for culture)

### 6. Waste Segregation
- **Regular trash acceptable for:** Respiratory secretions alone, non-bloody items
- **Biohazard (red bag) required for:** ANY blood contamination, sharps, pathological waste
- **Key distinction:** Respiratory secretions ≠ biohazard waste (efficient resource management)

### 7. Patient Visit Sequencing
**Correct order:** Clean → contact → droplet → airborne
- Prevents bringing high-contamination pathogens to vulnerable unprotected patients
- Hand hygiene required between each patient

### 8. Occupational Exposure Response
- **Time-critical:** PEP for bloodborne pathogen exposure most effective within 2 hours
- **Sequence:** Report to occupational health IMMEDIATELY → baseline labs → PEP evaluation → wound care → documentation
- **Reporting supersedes documentation:** Don't wait to file incident report before seeking occupational health evaluation
- **Assume positive risk** if patient status unknown; don't delay PEP waiting for results

## HESI High-Yield Patterns

| Pattern | Application | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Act on suspicion** | Implement precautions before diagnosis confirmed | Suspected TB = airborne precautions immediately |
| **Time-critical urgency** | Some actions have narrow intervention windows | PEP for needlestick (2-hour window) |
| **Context > rule** | Actions depend on circumstance, not blanket rules | Hand hygiene required for room entry even without patient touch |
| **Sequence matters** | Order of actions affects safety and efficiency | PPE doffing sequence; sterile field setup before glove donning |
| **Distinguish packages from contents** | Overcaution can be inefficient | Outer wrapper ≠ sterile field; touching package doesn't require discard |
| **Transmission routes guide precautions** | Know the pathogen's primary route | MRSA = contact (not respiratory); TB = airborne (not droplet) |
| **Spore exception** | Organisms with spore resistance need different methods | C. difficile requires soap/water (alcohol ineffective) |
| **Efficiency + safety** | Resource management matters in HESI | Respiratory waste to regular trash, not biohazard |

## Common HESI Traps (Student Avoided These)

| Trap | Why it's Wrong | Correct Answer |
|------|----------------|-----------------|
| "Wait for confirmation before precautions" | Delays exposure control | Implement immediately on suspicion |
| "Touch sterile wrapper = discard all" | Overcautious; wrapper is non-sterile | Continue; wrapper contact acceptable |
| "MRSA needs respiratory precautions" | Wrong transmission route | MRSA is contact (gloves/gown), not airborne |
| "Report incident BEFORE occupational health" | Wrong priority; PEP time-critical | Report to occupational health FIRST |
| "Alcohol-based sanitizer works for C. diff" | Spores resistant to alcohol | Soap and water ONLY for C. difficile |
| "All patient care items = biohazard waste" | Overcautious disposal | Respiratory secretions only = regular trash |
| "Don sterile gloves BEFORE setting up field" | Wastes sterile glove time | Set up field first, then don gloves |
