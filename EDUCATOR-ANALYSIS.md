## Cognitive Demand Analysis

### High-Stakes Clinical Reasoning Questions
These questions require students to weigh multiple competing options and apply clinical judgment:

- **Q08** – Maslow prioritization across four patient scenarios
- **Q28** – Hospice comfort care (first intervention for dyspnea)
- **Q30** – UAP scope of practice with immediate patient safety implications
- **Q37** – End-of-life physical assessment interpretation
- **Q46** – Risk stratification for wound infection
- **Q47** – SBAR "Recommendation" component identification
- **Q50** – Identifying the primary reason a wound is not healing

### Common Misconception / Distractor Quality
Several questions are particularly well-constructed because the distractors reflect common nursing errors:

- **Q03**: Students may confuse medical asepsis with surgical asepsis (keeping supplies "below waist" is surgical, not medical, asepsis).
- **Q06**: Students may try to explain or encourage rather than verify the order first — a medication safety principle.
- **Q14**: Multiple distractors specify exact numeric pass counts, exploiting test-taking anxiety around remembering numbers.
- **Q25**: The 1-inch rule from the umbilicus is a fine-detail skill that is easy to misremember.
- **Q33**: Students may default to "oral" for temperature without considering the clinical context (dehydration + vomiting).
- **Q36**: Distractor options include mostly correct BP techniques, requiring students to identify the *incorrect* one.

## Format & Psychometric Observations

### SATA Question Design
- **Q01, Q18, Q21, Q44** are strong SATA questions because the distractors include plausible but incorrect procedures (e.g., clamping catheter tubing, using airborne precautions for droplet illness).
- **Q11** contains six options with four correct answers — a high cognitive load item that rewards understanding sleep hygiene principles holistically.
- **Q27** (clear liquid diet) is straightforward and primarily tests recall; it could be strengthened by including more ambiguous items (e.g., protein shakes, fruit punch).

### Negatively Phrased Items
- **Q36** uses a "which action should the nurse **avoid**" format, which tests understanding at a different cognitive level but can trip up students who skim.
- **Q22** asks which statement indicates a **need for further teaching** — a common NCLEX-style negative format.

### Consistency Issues Corrected
1. **Q03**, **Q12**, and **Q30** were previously labeled "Multiple choice" (SATA) but each has only one correct answer. The labels have been corrected to "Single choice" in this update.
2. Point values are uniform (2 points each), which is appropriate given comparable difficulty levels.

## Strengths of the Question Set

1. **Breadth**: Covers a wide range of fundamental nursing competencies relevant to entry-level practice.
2. **Clinical context**: Most questions are scenario-based, which reinforces application over memorization.
3. **NCLEX alignment**: Heavy use of "first action," "priority," and "best response" stems mirrors the NCLEX style students will face on licensure exams.
4. **SATA variety**: Twelve SATA items adequately prepare students for that question type, which historically carries lower pass rates on NCLEX.
5. **Answer explanation quality**: Q47 includes a supplementary SBAR note explaining how all four components fit together — a model for how all complex questions could be annotated.

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Add rationale notes** to every question (similar to Q47) explaining why each distractor is incorrect. This transforms the question set into a self-study tool.
3. **Increase difficulty gradient**: Currently most questions are at a similar difficulty level. Consider adding a few advanced-difficulty questions requiring multi-step reasoning (e.g., interpreting lab values in context).
4. **Expand the psychosocial domain**: Only 4 questions address end-of-life, grief, and therapeutic communication — an area heavily tested on NCLEX.
5. **Add pharmacology items**: Only 3 questions involve medication administration. Dosage calculation, medication rights, and adverse effect recognition are critical competency areas.
6. **Incorporate health assessment findings**: No questions address abnormal auscultation, percussion, or inspection findings — important components of the physical assessment domain.
