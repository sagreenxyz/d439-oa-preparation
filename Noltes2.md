## Unit 1: Nursing Process & Clinical Judgment

### 1.1 Overview: ADPIE

| Phase | Description |
|---|---|
| **Assessment** | Collect subjective and objective data |
| **Diagnosis** | Analyze data; identify actual or potential problems |
| **Planning** | Set measurable goals; select interventions |
| **Implementation** | Carry out planned interventions |
| **Evaluation** | Determine whether goals are met; modify plan as needed |

---

### 1.2 Assessment: Subjective vs. Objective Data

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Subjective** | What the client *reports*; cannot be independently verified | Pain rating, fear, verbal statements, describing excitement about discharge |
| **Objective** | What the nurse *observes or measures* | Temperature, wound appearance, pacing the floor, grimacing |

> 💡 Observable behaviors (pacing, grimacing) = **objective**. Verbal statements — even about emotions — = **subjective**.

---

### 1.3 Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs in Nursing

Primary use: **establishing priorities of care**.

**Five Levels (base → top):** Physiological → Safety → Love/Belonging → Esteem → Self-Actualization

**Application:**
- Oxygenation = highest-priority physiological need
- Acute shortness of breath (Level 1) > suicidal ideation (Level 2) > constipation (Level 1, less urgent) > social concerns (Level 3)
- Suicidal ideation (Level 2, Safety) > elimination (Level 1 but non-urgent) > social/esteem concerns

---

### 1.4 Nursing Diagnosis & Priority Setting

- **Actual:** Problem currently present (e.g., Impaired Skin Integrity)
- **Risk:** Vulnerability to a problem (e.g., Risk for Falls)
- Priority frameworks: Maslow's hierarchy + ABCs (Airway → Breathing → Circulation)

---

### 1.5 Planning & Goal Setting (SMART Goals)

Goals describe **client outcomes**, not nursing actions.
- ✅ "Client will maintain SpO₂ >92% on room air within 24 hours."
- ❌ "Nurse will administer oxygen as ordered."

---

### 1.6 Evaluation & Unmet Goals

When a goal is **not met:**
1. Reassess the client
2. Evaluate appropriateness of interventions
3. **Modify interventions** and/or reset timeline
4. Do **not** delete unmet goals — they remain active until resolved

## Unit 2: Legal & Ethical Concepts

### 2.1 Ethical Principles in Nursing

| Principle | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Autonomy** | Patient's right to self-determination |
| **Beneficence** | Acting in patient's best interest |
| **Non-maleficence** | Do no harm |
| **Justice** | Fair and equal treatment for all |
| **Fidelity** | Keeping promises; honoring commitments |
| **Veracity** | Truthfulness in communication |

---

### 2.2 Legal Concepts: Assault, Battery, Slander, Libel

| Term | Definition | Key Distinguisher |
|---|---|---|
| **Assault** | Threat causing fear of bodily harm; no physical contact required | *The threat* — "I won't answer your call bell" |
| **Battery** | Actual unwanted physical contact without consent | *The act* |
| **Slander** | *Spoken* false statement damaging reputation | Spoken defamation |
| **Libel** | *Written* false statement damaging reputation | Written defamation |

---

### 2.3 Informed Consent & Witnessing

**Nurse witness signature confirms ONLY:**
- The patient signed the form
- The nurse witnessed the signing

**Does NOT confirm:** Understanding, being unmedicated, freedom from pressure (provider's responsibility).

---

### 2.4 Confidentiality, HIPAA & Emancipated Minors

**Emancipated minors** = legally recognized as adults for medical decisions (via court order, marriage, military, parenthood, independent living in some states).

**Rights:** Consent to/receive care independently; full HIPAA confidentiality; **no parental notification** required without the minor's written consent.

---

### 2.5 Advance Directives & DNR

- If no advance directive: **offer education and provide the form**
- If patient requests no CPR: **notify provider** to write the DNR order — nurse cannot implement without a written order
- If advance directive conflicts with family: **advocate for patient's expressed wishes** — advance directive takes legal precedence

---

### 2.6 Good Samaritan Law

Protects voluntary, good-faith, uncompensated emergency care at the scene.

**Protected:** CPR at accident scene while off duty; staying until EMS arrives.
**Not protected:** Phone advice; transporting in personal vehicle; accepting payment.

## Unit 3: Communication & Therapeutic Relationships

### 3.1 Therapeutic Communication Techniques

**Effective:** Active listening; open-ended questions ("Tell me more..."); reflection; clarification; silence; empathy.

**Non-therapeutic (avoid):** False reassurance ("Everything will be fine"); minimizing ("It's probably for the best"); opinions ("You shouldn't feel that way"); changing the subject.

---

### 3.2 Phases of the Therapeutic Relationship

| Phase | Primary Activity |
|---|---|
| **Orientation** | Build rapport; introduce self; begin assessment |
| **Working** | Interventions; goal-directed interaction; teaching |
| **Termination** | Review progress; provide closure |

> 💡 Orientation phase = **building rapport** — not implementing interventions.

---

### 3.3 Communication with Special Populations

**Language barriers:** Always use a **professional medical interpreter** — never a family member or untrained staff. Speaking louder/slower does not improve comprehension.

**Suicidal ideation:** Open-ended, non-judgmental. Best: "Describe what you are experiencing." Avoid "why" questions — feel accusatory.

**Anxiety:** "Tell me more about what's making you uneasy."

**Cultural sensitivity:** Using family as translators is NOT best practice in clinical settings, despite feeling supportive.

---

### 3.4 SBAR Communication Framework

| Component | Content |
|---|---|
| **S — Situation** | What is happening right now |
| **B — Background** | Relevant clinical context |
| **A — Assessment** | Nurse's clinical judgment |
| **R — Recommendation** | Specific action requested from provider |

---

## Unit 4: Documentation

### 4.1 Principles of Accurate Documentation

Accurate, timely, objective, complete. Best practice example:
> "Suctioned tracheostomy with 12 Fr catheter ×2; thick yellow secretions obtained; SpO₂ remained >95%."

Preferred over vague entries like "Client tolerated suctioning well."

---

### 4.2 Downtime Documentation

When EHR is unavailable:
- **Document on paper using facility downtime forms** — do not delay
- Enter into EHR once restored with late-entry notation
- Never rely solely on memory

---

## Unit 5: Patient Education

### 5.1 Principles of Patient Teaching

| Method | Best Used For |
|---|---|
| **Return demonstration** | Psychomotor skills (injections, wound care) |
| **Role-play** | Confidence building; decision-making skills; active participation |
| **Verbal instruction** | Foundational knowledge; supplement with other methods |
| **Written materials** | Reinforcement and reference |

**Most effective for insulin injection teaching:** Return demonstration using a practice device (e.g., orange).

---

### 5.2 Evaluating Patient Understanding

**Gold standard = return demonstration or teach-back.**

| Response | Demonstrates Understanding? |
|---|---|
| Patient nods during explanation | ❌ No |
| Patient says "I understand" | ❌ No |
| **Patient correctly demonstrates the skill** | ✅ **Yes — gold standard** |
| Patient says they'll look it up online | ❌ No |

## Unit 6: Vital Signs

### 6.1 Normal Adult Vital Signs

| Parameter | Normal Range |
|---|---|
| Temperature | 36.1–37.2°C (97–99°F) |
| Pulse | 60–100 beats/min |
| Respiratory rate | 12–20 breaths/min |
| Blood pressure | <120/<80 mmHg |
| SpO₂ | 95–100% |

---

### 6.2 Temperature Assessment

| Route | Best Used For | Key Points |
|---|---|---|
| **Oral** | Alert, cooperative adults | Preferred for alert adults; avoid after recent oral intake |
| **Rectal** | Infants; unconscious | Most accurate core temperature |
| **Tympanic** | Children and adults; rapid | Adults: pinna **up and back**; children <3: pinna **down and back**; seal at canal opening, not deep |
| **Axillary** | Least invasive | Least accurate; probe in **center of axilla**; hold arm close to body |

---

### 6.3 Pulse Assessment

- **Radial** — routine adults
- **Apical** — irregular rhythm; child <2 years; cardiac medications
- Count **60 seconds** when rhythm is irregular
- **Never use the thumb** — has its own pulsation

---

### 6.4 Respiratory Rate Assessment

- Count **after** pulse while appearing to still assess pulse
- Count **1 full minute** for bradypnea or irregular rhythms
- **RR 10, irregular → immediate report** (below normal AND irregular rhythm)

---

### 6.5 Blood Pressure Assessment

- Arm at **heart level**; seated quietly **≥5 minutes**
- Cuff encircles **≥80%** of limb
- **No caffeine/nicotine within 30 minutes** (not 15 — exam trap)

---

## Unit 7: Health Assessment & Physical Examination

### 7.2 Pain Assessment: The PQRST Method

| Letter | Category | Sample Question |
|---|---|---|
| **P** | Provocation/Palliation | "What makes the pain better or worse?" |
| **Q** | Quality | "Is it sharp, dull, burning, aching?" |
| **R** | Region/Radiation | "Where is the pain? Does it radiate?" |
| **S** | Severity | "On a scale of 0–10, how severe is your pain?" |
| **T** | Timing | "Is it constant or intermittent? When does it start?" |

> 💡 **S = Severity = Scale (0–10)**. Q = Quality/character (sharp, dull, burning).

## Unit 8: Infection Prevention and Control

### 8.1 Overview of Infection Control

**Infection control** refers to policies, procedures, and practices used to prevent the spread of infectious agents in healthcare settings. HAIs are largely preventable.

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Infection** | Invasion/multiplication of pathogen in body tissue producing signs/symptoms |
| **HAI** | Infection acquired during healthcare; not present at admission |
| **Colonization** | Microorganisms present without causing signs of infection |
| **Pathogen** | Microorganism capable of causing disease |
| **Virulence** | Degree of pathogenicity; ability to cause disease |

---

### 8.2 The Chain of Infection

Breaking **any single link** prevents transmission.

| Link | Definition | Nursing Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Infectious Agent** | The pathogen | Antimicrobials; disinfection; sterilization |
| **2. Reservoir** | Where pathogen lives/multiplies | Environmental cleaning; waste disposal |
| **3. Portal of Exit** | How pathogen leaves reservoir | Wound care; respiratory hygiene; sharps disposal |
| **4. Mode of Transmission** | How it travels to host | Hand hygiene; PPE; isolation |
| **5. Portal of Entry** | How it enters new host | Sterile technique; catheter care; skin integrity |
| **6. Susceptible Host** | Person lacking resistance | Immunizations; nutrition; managing chronic illness |

> 💡 **Hand hygiene is the single most effective measure** for breaking the chain of infection.

---

### 8.3 Modes of Transmission

- **Contact** (most common HAI mode) — direct (person-to-person) or indirect (via contaminated surface/object)
- **Droplet** — droplets >5 microns; travel ≤3 feet; land on mucous membranes
- **Airborne** — droplet nuclei ≤5 microns; remain suspended; travel long distances
- **Vehicle** — contaminated food, water, medications, or IV fluids
- **Vector-borne** — via animal or insect intermediary

---

### 8.4 Standard Precautions

Applied to **all patients at all times** — treats all blood/body fluids as potentially infectious.

- **Hand hygiene** — before/after every patient contact; after removing gloves; after touching patient environment
- **PPE** — gloves (blood/body fluid contact); gown (soiling risk); mask + eye protection (splash risk)
- **Safe sharps handling** — never recap; dispose in puncture-resistant container
- **Respiratory hygiene/cough etiquette**
- **Safe injection practices** — never reuse needles or syringes

---

### 8.5 Transmission-Based Precautions

Used *in addition to* standard precautions.

| Category | Mode | Common Pathogens | PPE | Room |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Contact** | Direct/indirect contact | MRSA, VRE, *C. diff*, scabies, RSV | Gloves + Gown on entry | Private or cohort |
| **Droplet** | Droplets >5 microns | Influenza, COVID-19, pertussis, mumps, RSV | Surgical mask within 3 ft | Private preferred |
| **Airborne** | Droplet nuclei ≤5 microns | TB, measles, varicella | Fit-tested N95 | Negative-pressure room |

**RSV** requires **both contact AND droplet precautions** — gown, gloves, surgical mask within 3 feet, hand hygiene, private room preferred. Negative-pressure room is NOT required.

**Key Notes:**
- PPE donned *before* entering; doffed *outside* the room
- *C. difficile* requires soap and water — alcohol-based rubs do **not** kill *C. diff* spores

---

### 8.6 Aseptic Technique

#### Medical Asepsis (Clean Technique)

Reduces and limits microorganisms. Used for routine care not involving penetration of sterile body cavities.

**Core Principles:**
1. Hand hygiene foundation — wash at least **20 seconds**
2. Clean to dirty — always move from cleanest to most contaminated
3. Hold soiled items away from body; discard directly into receptacles
4. Items are clean, not sterile

**CDC Hand Hygiene Steps:** Wet → soap → lather 20 sec (backs, between fingers, under nails) → rinse → dry → use towel to turn off faucet

#### Surgical Asepsis (Sterile Technique)

Complete elimination of all microorganisms including spores. Required for procedures penetrating skin or entering sterile body cavities.

**Key Principles:**
- Only sterile items placed within sterile field
- Sterile objects contaminated when touched by non-sterile objects
- Sterile fields out of nurse's line of vision = contaminated
- Wet/damp sterile fields = contaminated (wicking)
- Outer **1-inch border** of sterile drape = non-sterile
- Sterile supplies held **above waist level** — below waist = contaminated
- Pour sterile solutions without touching bottle to field; discard cap once opened
- Never turn back to or reach across sterile field
- Open sterile packages **away from the body**

| Feature | Medical Asepsis | Surgical Asepsis |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Reduce microorganisms | Eliminate ALL microorganisms |
| Equipment | Clean | Sterile |
| Gloves | Clean | Sterile |
| Examples | Wound cleaning, oral care, catheter bag emptying | Catheter insertion, IV placement, surgery |

## Unit 9: Patient Safety & Restraints

### 9.1 Overview

Patient safety encompasses all measures to prevent harm during healthcare delivery.

---

### 9.2 Restraint Use: Principles & Guidelines (TJC)

- Used only **after all alternatives have been attempted and failed**
- Requires a **provider order**
- **Two-finger rule** — confirms appropriate fit; prevents circulatory compromise
- Tied to the **bed frame** — **never** to side rails
- **Reassess every 2 hours** — skin integrity, circulation, ROM
- **24-hour order renewal** required
- **Never** for punishment or staff convenience

**Priority sequence:**

2. Notify the healthcare provider
3. Complete incident report

### 10.4 Heparin Subcutaneous Injection

- Site: fatty abdomen; **at least 2 inches (5 cm)** from umbilicus
- **Do NOT massage** after injection
- **Rotate sites** with each dose
- Do not aspirate

> 💡 **Most tested error:** Massaging the site after injection.
> **Second most tested:** Injecting only 1 inch from umbilicus — must be **≥2 inches**.

---

### 10.5 Ear Drop Administration

| Age | Pinna Technique |
|---|---|
| Adult (≥3 years) | **Up and back** |
| Child (<3 years) | **Down and back** |

After administration: side-lying with affected ear **up** for 2–3 min. Warm to **room temperature** — cold drops cause vertigo.

## Unit 11: Hygiene & Skin Care

### 11.1 Overview

Nurses assist patients who cannot perform hygiene independently and implement measures to prevent skin breakdown.

---

### 11.2 Oral Care for Unconscious Patients

**Before oral care:** Assess the **gag reflex** — absence increases aspiration risk.

**Technique:** Lateral position; suction available; small amounts of liquid; foam swabs or soft toothbrush.

---

### 11.3 Skin Care for Older Adults

**Aging skin changes:** Decreased sebaceous gland activity → dry, flaky skin; thinning epidermis; reduced collagen; decreased subcutaneous fat.

**Best intervention for dry, flaky skin:**
- Bathe with **warm (not hot) water** + apply **emollient** after bathing
- **Avoid:** Alcohol-based cleansers; talcum powder; frequent soap use

---

### 11.4 Foot Care for Diabetic Patients

- **Assess feet daily** — inspect for cuts, blisters, redness, swelling
- Wear **cotton socks and supportive footwear** at all times
- **Cut toenails straight across** — prevents ingrown toenails
- **Never walk barefoot**
- **No hot water soaks** — impaired sensation prevents detecting burns
- **No lotion between toes** — promotes fungal growth

---

### 11.5 Warm Compress Application

**Before applying warm compress, assess:**
- **Sensation** — can the patient feel heat? (impaired sensation → burn risk)
- **Circulation** — is blood flow adequate?

Apply max **20 minutes**; wrap in cloth; never apply directly to skin.

---

## Unit 12: Skin Integrity & Wound Care

### 12.1 Pressure Injury Staging

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| **Stage I** | Intact skin; **non-blanchable redness** over bony prominence |
| **Stage II** | **Partial-thickness** dermis loss; shallow open ulcer with red-pink bed, OR intact/ruptured blister |
| **Stage III** | **Full-thickness** tissue loss; subcutaneous fat visible; **no** bone/tendon/muscle exposed; slough may be present |
| **Stage IV** | Full-thickness loss; **bone, tendon, or muscle exposed** |
| **Unstageable** | Full-thickness; base obscured by slough/eschar — cannot stage until debrided |

**Stage III findings:** Full-thickness loss + visible SubQ fat + no bone/tendon/muscle + slough/eschar may be present.
**Not Stage III:** Intact skin + redness = Stage I; shallow open ulcer + red-pink bed = Stage II; blister = Stage II; exposed bone = Stage IV.

---

### 12.2 Braden Scale

Scores 6–23 (lower = higher risk).

| Score | Risk Level | Recommended Action |
|---|---|---|
| ≤9 | Very high | Intensive prevention protocol |
| **10–12** | **High** | **Foam mattress overlay + turn every 2 hours** |
| 13–14 | Moderate | Standard prevention measures |
| ≥19 | No risk | Routine care |

---

### 12.3 Wound Healing: Types & Intention

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary intention** | Clean edges approximated; minimal tissue loss; minimal scarring | Surgical incision, kitchen knife cut |
| **Secondary intention** | Large tissue loss; heals from base up with granulation tissue | Pressure ulcers, large abrasions |
| **Tertiary (delayed primary)** | Left open initially; closed later | Contaminated wounds |

**Stage IV pressure ulcer → secondary intention (full-thickness wound repair).**

---

### 12.4 Wound Drainage Types

| Type | Appearance | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Serous** | Clear, watery | Normal early drainage |
| **Sanguineous** | Bright red, bloody | Active bleeding |
| **Serosanguineous** | Pink; mix of serous + blood | Normal in healing wounds |
| **Purulent** | Thick, yellow/green, foul odor | **Infection** |

---

### 12.5 Risk Factors for Wound Infection

**Highest risk:** Surgical creation of a colostomy — bowel (highly contaminated) brought to surface; direct fecal contamination of surgical wound.

---

### 12.6 Factors Affecting Wound Healing

**Most significant nutritional factor: Protein** — essential for collagen synthesis, immune response, tissue repair.
- Vitamin D deficiency: modest role; not primary
- Massaging pressure injuries: **contraindicated** — increases tissue damage

---

### 12.7 Skin Preparation for Topical Medications

Before applying a prescription ointment:
- **Clean with soap and water, rinse, and pat dry**
- Do not use alcohol (dries/irritates)
- Do not skip cleaning (removes old medication and debris)
- Sterile gloves not required for routine topical medications

### 13.3 Oxygen Safety

- Remove all **petroleum-based/flammable materials**
- Post **"No Smoking" signs**
- Use **cotton bedding** over synthetic
- Ensure electrical equipment **properly grounded**
- Keep tanks **secured upright**
- **Never** let client adjust flow rate independently
- Use water-soluble (not petroleum-based) lubricant near oxygen

### 13.4 Nursing Care: Oxygen Therapy & MDRPIs

Nasal cannula MDRPIs at nares and behind ears:
- **Pad tubing** with gauze or foam
- Apply **water-soluble lubricant** to nares

- **Never tighten** — increases pressure

---

### 13.5 Suctioning

- **Pre-oxygenation** = highest priority before suctioning
- Suction **only during withdrawal**, not insertion
- Each pass ≤**10–15 seconds**; allow recovery between passes
- No routine saline instillation
- Dispose catheter in **red biohazard bin**

**SpO₂ drop from 96% to 88% after suctioning → requires immediate action.**

### 13.6 Obstructive Sleep Apnea & CPAP

**Highest risk:** Male, BMI ≥35, hypertension.

**CPAP must be used every night indefinitely** — stopping when feeling better is INCORRECT.

| Calcium (Ca²⁺) | 8.5–10.5 mg/dL |

### 14.2 Common Electrolyte Imbalances

**Hypernatremia (Na⁺ >145 mEq/L):**
- Signs: dry mucous membranes, intense thirst, confusion
- **Sign of improvement:** Serum sodium returning to 136–145 mEq/L

**Hyponatremia / Water Intoxication:**
- Psychogenic polydipsia (>3 L/day of water) — seen in schizophrenia
- Priority concern: **risk for water intoxication** (not dehydration)
- Cells absorb excess water → cerebral edema → **decreased LOC** = urgent

### 14.3 IV Fluid Types

|---|---|---|---|
| **Isotonic** | Same as plasma | No change | 0.9% NS, Lactated Ringer's |
| **Hypotonic** | Less than plasma | Cells swell | 0.45% NS |
| **Hypertonic** | Greater than plasma | Cells shrink | 3% NS, D10W |

## Unit 15: Pain Management

### 15.1 Overview of Pain

**Types:** Acute (short duration, tissue damage); Chronic (>3–6 months); Nociceptive (somatic/visceral); Neuropathic (nerve damage — burning, shooting, tingling).

Pain is subjective — **patient self-report is the gold standard**.

---

### 15.2 Nursing Assessment of Pain

Use PQRST + numeric scale (0–10).

**Nonverbal pain indicators:** Grimacing, guarding, restlessness, elevated vitals, splinting.

When nonverbal signs are present → **ask the client directly** if they are experiencing pain. Never wait for a verbal report.

---

### 15.3 Pharmacological Pain Management

**After administering opioid analgesics:**
- Reassess pain in **30–60 minutes** (IV: 15–30 min)
- If pain goal not met → notify provider; reassess plan

**Opioid toxicity signs:** Shallow respirations, excessive drowsiness, pinpoint pupils, decreased LOC.
**Priority action:** **Stop the PCA pump** and notify provider immediately.

---

### 15.4 Non-Pharmacological Pain Management

Independent nursing interventions (no order required):
- Guided imagery
- Deep breathing exercises
- Warm compress
- Repositioning
- Distraction (music, TV)
- **Cold/ice** — reduces inflammation and numbs pain; apply max **20 minutes**

> 💡 Ice = reduces inflammation + numbs. Heat = vasodilation + muscle relaxation.

---

### 15.5 Patient-Controlled Analgesia (PCA)

**Only the patient presses the PCA button.** Family/proxy pressing bypasses safety lockout and risks overdose.

**Action if family presses PCA:** Educate family immediately that only the patient should press the button.

---

## Unit 16: Sleep

### 16.1 Overview of Sleep

Essential physiological need. Hospital sleep disruptors: noise, light, pain, frequent procedures, medications (steroids, diuretics), altered routines.

---

### 16.2 Promoting Restorative Sleep in Hospitalized Patients

**Appropriate interventions:**
- Back massage before bedtime
- Reduce environmental noise; dim lights
- Avoid administering diuretics late in the evening
- Manage pain before bedtime
- Cluster nursing activities to minimize awakenings

**Inappropriate interventions:**
- Encouraging TV until sleep — stimulating; delays sleep onset
- Waking every 2 hours routinely — disruptive; not evidence-based as a sleep intervention

## Unit 17: Nutrition & Diet Therapy

### 17.1 Overview of Nutrition

**Key nutrient for wound healing: Protein** — required for collagen synthesis, immune function, and tissue repair. Inadequate protein = most common nutritional cause of pressure ulcer failure to heal.

---

### 17.2 Therapeutic Diets: Clear Liquid Diet

**Allowed:**
- Broth (chicken, beef, vegetable)
- Clear fruit juices without pulp (apple, white grape, cranberry)
- Gelatin (Jell-O)
- Clear popsicles (fruit-flavored, no pulp, no cream) ✅
- Coffee and tea (without cream)
- Sports drinks, carbonated beverages

**NOT allowed:**
- Milk, cream, ice cream ❌
- Pudding, yogurt ❌
- Vegetable juice (opaque) ❌
- Fruit juice with pulp ❌

> 💡 Popsicles **are** allowed if clear/transparent with no cream or pulp. Pudding and ice cream are NOT.

---

### 17.3 Enteral Nutrition & Nasogastric Tubes

**Aspiration prevention during enteral feeding:**
- Position: **semi-Fowler's (HOB 30–45°)** during and for 30–60 min after feeding
- Never feed in supine, Trendelenburg, or left lateral position

**NG Tube Insertion:**
1. Position client in **semi-Fowler's** before insertion
2. Measure: **nose → earlobe → xiphoid process**
3. Verify placement by **X-ray** (gold standard)
4. Auscultation of air alone = not reliable for verification

**Home NGT Safety Teaching:**
- Elevate HOB **at least 30°** during and after feedings
- Tape tube **securely** (not loosely)
- Wash hands **before and after** handling NGT

## Unit 18: Activity, Immobility & Patient Positioning

### 18.1 Overview

Patient positioning = deliberate body placement to promote comfort, prevent complications, support physiology, and facilitate recovery.

---

### 18.2 Common Therapeutic Positions

| Position | HOB Angle | Primary Indications |
|---|---|---|
| **Fowler's** | 45–60° | Respiratory distress, cardiac conditions, feeding |
| **Semi-Fowler's** | 15–45° (typically 30°) | Post-op recovery, enteral feeding, aspiration prevention |
| **High Fowler's** | 60–90° | Severe dyspnea, pulmonary edema |
| **Supine** | Flat | Spinal procedures, post-anesthesia recovery |
| **Prone** | Face down | ARDS prone ventilation, spinal surgery recovery |
| **Lateral** | Side | Pressure injury prevention, unconscious patients |
| **Sims'** | Semi-prone | Enemas, rectal exams, unconscious patients |
| **Trendelenburg** | Head lower | Postural drainage |
| **Reverse Trendelenburg** | Head higher (whole bed tilted) | GERD, hiatal hernia |
| **Lithotomy** | Hips/knees flexed, legs in stirrups | Pelvic/gynecological procedures |

---

### 18.3 Semi-Fowler's Position: Indications & Rationale

**Primary benefit: Respiratory support** — diaphragm descends freely; increases lung expansion. Critical after abdominal surgery (abdominal contents push up against diaphragm when supine).

Secondary benefits: wound drainage by gravity; aspiration prevention; comfort.

---

### 18.4 Therapeutic Communication in Positioning

When explaining semi-Fowler's to post-surgical patient who prefers lying flat:
> "Raising the head of your bed supports your breathing."

Lead with the most clinically significant and relevant benefit.

---

### 18.5 Types of Exercise

| Category | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Isotonic** | Muscle contraction WITH movement; joint angle changes | Bicycling, swimming, walking, jogging, dancing |
| **Isometric** | Muscle contraction WITHOUT movement; no joint angle change | Quad sets, gluteal contractions, tensing muscles in place |
| **Resistive isometric** | Contraction against resistance; no ROM | Push-ups, hip lifts, pushing feet against footboard |

> 💡 Isotonic = movement occurs. Isometric = no movement. Push-ups/hip lifts = resistive isometric (not isotonic).

---

### 18.6 Range-of-Motion Exercises

- Support joints **above and below** the joint being moved
- Move limbs **slowly and smoothly**
- **Stop if client reports pain**
- When client says "too tired to go on": **stop, decrease reps, assess for other symptoms, re-evaluate care plan**
- Perform ROM on **both sides** for hemiparesis — not only the unaffected side
- Do **not** hyperextend joints

---

### 18.7 Assistive Devices: Cane & Crutch Use

**Cane Use with Hemiparesis:**
- Hold cane in the **strong (unaffected) hand**
- Elbow flexed at approximately **15–30°** when gripping
- Gait: cane and weak leg advance **simultaneously**, then strong leg follows
- Do not lean heavily on cane

**Crutch Fitting:**
- **2–3 finger widths** between axilla and crutch pad — prevents axillary nerve compression
- Elbow bent **15–30°** at hand grips
- Hand grips at level of **greater trochanter** (hip level)
- Weight borne through **hands**, NOT axillae — axillary weight-bearing causes "crutch palsy" (brachial plexus injury)

---

### 18.8 Safe Ambulation & Fall Prevention

- **Nonslip-soled shoes** — never socks alone
- Use a **gait belt** for unsteady clients
- Keep bed in **lowest position** for fall-risk patients
- **1:1 sitter** for confused clients attempting to get out of bed

---

### 18.9 Log-Rolling Technique

Used for spinal instability (e.g., post-spinal surgery).

**Correct technique:**
- **Head, shoulders, and hips must remain in perfect alignment** throughout
- Requires ≥2 nurses moving as one unit — not in segments
- **Drawsheet is permitted** (NOT contraindicated)
- Legs remain **straight** (not bent)

### 19.3 Factors Affecting Urinary Elimination

### 19.4 Urinary Catheterization

| Type | Description | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Intermittent (straight)** | Inserted then immediately removed | Retention, sterile specimen |

| **Suprapubic** | Through abdominal wall | Long-term drainage |

### 19.5 Indwelling Catheters & CAUTI Prevention

- Maintain **closed drainage system** — never disconnect
- Keep bag **below bladder level** at all times
- **Daily meatal hygiene** with soap and water
- **Secure catheter to inner thigh**
- Assess necessity **daily** — remove ASAP
- **Never clamp** catheter

---

### 19.6 Urine Specimen Collection

**Clean-catch:** Refrigerate or put on ice **immediately** — room-temperature storage allows bacterial growth.

**Sterile specimen from indwelling catheter:** Collect from **specimen port** on tubing — NOT from the drainage bag.

**24-hour urine collection:**
- **Discard** first morning void; begin timing from that point
- Collect **all** urine for 24 hours including nighttime
- Keep **refrigerated/on ice** throughout
- If any urine accidentally discarded: **notify the laboratory** — collection may need to restart
- Final void at end of 24 hours is **added** to the collection

### 21.1 Signs of Imminent Death

| Finding | Significance |
|---|---|
| **Cold, clammy, wax-like skin** | **Peripheral circulatory shutdown → imminent death** |
| Mottling of extremities | Poor peripheral circulation |
| Cheyne-Stokes respirations | Alternating apnea and hyperpnea |

### 21.2 Comfort Care & Symptom Management

For terminally ill patient with dyspnea (advanced heart failure on hospice):

**Priority order:**
1. **Elevate head of bed** — immediate, independent; improves respiratory mechanics
2. Sublingual morphine (PRN) — reduces air hunger after positioning
3. IV furosemide (PRN) — for fluid overload; requires assessment + order

### Question 1 of 50 — CAUTI Prevention

**Topic:** Urinary Elimination | **Concept:** CAUTI Prevention

**Question:** A nurse is providing care to a client with an indwelling urinary catheter. Which actions should the nurse perform to prevent CAUTI? *(Select all that apply)*

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Keep the drainage bag below the level of the bladder at all times | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Clamp the catheter tubing every 4 hours to allow the bladder to fill | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Clean the catheter insertion site daily with soap and water | ✅ YES |
| 4 | Disconnect the catheter tubing frequently for cleaning | ❌ NO |
| 5 | Secure the catheter to the client's thigh to prevent tension on the tubing | ✅ YES |

**Correct answers: 1, 3, 5**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** The drainage bag must always remain below the level of the bladder. Gravity drives urine flow from the bladder into the bag. If the bag is raised above bladder level — even briefly during transport — urine flows backward into the bladder, seeding it with bacteria. This is one of the most direct and preventable mechanisms of CAUTI.

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** Clamping the catheter tubing to allow the bladder to fill is a harmful practice. Urine that remains stagnant in the bladder becomes a culture medium for bacterial growth. Catheters should drain freely and continuously. There is no clinical benefit to intermittent clamping of an indwelling catheter for routine care. *Note: Clamping prior to removal is sometimes referenced in older literature but is not supported by current evidence-based guidelines.*

✅ **Option 3 — Correct:** The urethral meatus and surrounding perineal area are the primary external entry points for bacteria traveling extraluminally along the catheter into the bladder. Cleaning this area daily with soap and water — and after every bowel movement — reduces the periurethral bacterial burden and is a core component of the evidence-based CAUTI prevention bundle.

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** The catheter, tubing, and drainage bag form a closed sterile circuit. Every disconnection creates an open portal through which environmental bacteria can enter the internal lumen of the catheter and ascend into the bladder (intraluminal transmission). There is no clinical indication to disconnect tubing for "cleaning." The system should only be opened for specimen collection at the specimen port or when changing the drainage bag per protocol.

✅ **Option 5 — Correct:** Securing the catheter to the inner thigh (for female patients) or lower abdomen or thigh (for male patients) prevents the catheter from pulling at the insertion site. Tension and movement at the urethral meatus increase mechanical trauma, irritation, and the risk of periurethral bacteria being introduced into the urinary tract. Proper securement is a required component of catheter care.

> 💡 **#1 CAUTI Prevention:** Remove the catheter as soon as it is no longer clinically indicated. Every day the catheter remains in place increases infection risk — daily necessity assessment is essential.

### Question 2 of 50 — Oxygen Therapy & Skin Integrity

**Topic:** Oxygenation | **Concept:** Nasal Cannula; MDRPI Prevention; Independent Nursing Interventions

**Question:** A nurse notes redness and irritation around the client's nares and behind the ears from a nasal cannula at 4 L/min. Which is the priority nursing action?

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Apply a water-soluble lubricant to the client's nostrils and document the finding | ❌ NO |
| 2 | Secure the cannula tubing more tightly to prevent movement and dislodgement | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Notify the healthcare provider and request an order for humidified oxygen | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Pad the tubing with gauze or foam and reassess skin frequently for breakdown | ✅ YES |

**Correct answer: 4**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** Applying water-soluble lubricant to the nares is appropriate and does relieve mucosal dryness caused by oxygen flow. However, this action addresses only the dryness component of the irritation — it does not address the mechanical pressure being exerted by the tubing on the nares and behind the ears. Lubrication alone is an incomplete response when a device-related pressure injury is developing. Additionally, documenting and moving on does not intervene actively enough. *Note: Lubricant application is a valid supplemental action, but it is not the priority.*

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** Tightening the cannula tubing would worsen the problem significantly. The redness and irritation are caused by sustained pressure from the tubing against delicate skin. Increasing the tightness increases the pressure, directly accelerating tissue breakdown and injury. This option is not only incorrect — it is actively harmful and should never be done.

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** Humidified oxygen is appropriate and recommended at flow rates ≥4 L/min because dry oxygen irritates the nasal mucosa. However, requesting humidification requires a provider order — it is a dependent nursing action. The nurse must first take immediate independent action to address the current skin injury before escalating to the provider. Calling the provider before attempting independent interventions represents a failure to apply the nursing process correctly. *Note: Notifying the provider about worsening skin breakdown would be the correct next step if independent interventions fail.*

✅ **Option 4 — Correct:** Padding the tubing with gauze or foam at the sites of contact (nares and behind the ears) redistributes the pressure, protects the skin surface, and prevents further breakdown. This is an **independent nursing intervention** — it requires no provider order and can be performed immediately. Frequent reassessment of skin integrity allows the nurse to monitor the response and catch any progression toward ulceration early. This is the correct priority action because it directly addresses the cause of injury, falls within the nurse's independent scope, and can be implemented without delay.

### Question 3 of 50 — Medical Asepsis & Wound Care

**Topic:** Infection Prevention and Control | **Concept:** Medical Asepsis; Surgical Asepsis; Hand Hygiene

**Question:** A nurse is preparing to perform wound care using medical asepsis. Which action should the nurse take?

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Wash hands for at least 20 seconds before the procedure | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Keep sterile supplies below waist level | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Pour sterile solution onto a sterile field | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Touch the inside of the sterile dressing wrapper while applying the dressing | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 1**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** Hand hygiene for at least 20 seconds with soap and water is the cornerstone of medical (clean) asepsis. It reduces the microbial load on the hands to minimize pathogen transfer to the patient and the wound. The CDC recommends lathering all surfaces of the hands — backs, between fingers, under nails — for a minimum of 20 seconds. This is the defining action of medical aseptic technique.

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** This option states the opposite of a surgical asepsis principle and is factually wrong regardless of context. In surgical asepsis, sterile supplies must be held **above** waist level at all times — anything below waist level is considered contaminated because it passes out of the nurse's direct line of vision. This option is a distractor designed to test whether students can identify this principle correctly. *Note: This would be a violation of surgical aseptic technique. It is not applicable to medical asepsis, where sterile fields and strict waist-level rules do not apply.*

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** Establishing and maintaining a sterile field, then pouring sterile solution onto it, is a component of **surgical asepsis** — not medical asepsis. Under medical (clean) technique, a sterile field is never created. Pouring sterile solution is used in procedures such as sterile wound irrigation, urinary catheter insertion, and central line dressing changes — all of which use surgical aseptic technique. *Note: If the question were about surgical asepsis, this would be a potentially correct action — provided the bottle does not contact the field and is held properly.*

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** The inside of a sterile dressing wrapper or package is considered a sterile surface. Touching it with bare or clean-gloved hands contaminates the sterile contents. This violates the principles of **surgical asepsis** even though the question describes a medical asepsis context — under either level of technique, a nurse should not contaminate packaged sterile supplies. This option is incorrect both because it describes poor technique universally and because it introduces a surgical asepsis concept into a medical asepsis scenario.

### Question 4 of 50 — Patient Positioning: Semi-Fowler's

**Topic:** Activity, Immobility & Patient Positioning | **Concept:** Semi-Fowler's; Post-Operative Care; Therapeutic Communication

**Question:** A client post-abdominal surgery says, "I prefer to lie flat when in bed. I feel like I am almost sitting up." Which is the most appropriate response?

| # | Response | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Raising the head of your bed supports your breathing." | ✅ YES |
| 2 | "Keeping your upper body elevated encourages urinary elimination." | ❌ NO |
| 3 | "Increasing the height of the top of the bed promotes your passing gas." | ❌ NO |
| 4 | "Lifting your chest above your abdomen facilitates drainage in your wound drains." | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 1**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** The primary therapeutic benefit of semi-Fowler's position after abdominal surgery is **respiratory support**. When a post-abdominal surgery patient lies flat, the abdominal contents press upward against the diaphragm due to gravity and postoperative distension. This restricts diaphragmatic movement and reduces tidal volume, making breathing more labored. Elevating the head of the bed allows the diaphragm to descend fully with each breath, increasing lung expansion and reducing the work of breathing. This response uses plain language, directly addresses the patient's concern, and names the most immediately significant clinical benefit.

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** Semi-Fowler's position has no established clinical benefit for urinary elimination. Urinary elimination is influenced by fluid intake, bladder pressure, neurological function, sphincter tone, and mobility — not by head-of-bed elevation. Telling the patient this would be factually inaccurate and potentially misleading. *Note: If a patient were receiving enteral tube feedings or had gastroesophageal reflux, semi-Fowler's would be appropriate — but for aspiration prevention, not urinary function.*

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** While positioning and ambulation can promote the return of bowel function after abdominal surgery, semi-Fowler's position is not specifically indicated or recognized for promoting the passage of flatus. This benefit, even if there were any minor supporting evidence, is not the primary rationale and would not be the most appropriate explanation to give to a patient. Prioritizing this response over the respiratory benefit reflects a misunderstanding of the position's primary purpose.

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** Elevation of the upper body does use gravity to facilitate dependent wound drainage — fluid drains away from the wound site and toward collection systems. This is a real, secondary benefit of semi-Fowler's positioning. However, it is not the **primary** rationale, particularly for a patient who just had abdominal surgery. Additionally, the phrasing "lifting your chest above your abdomen" is anatomically awkward and may be confusing or alarming to the patient. The respiratory benefit is more clinically significant and more immediately relevant to the patient's recovery. *Note: If the question asked about a patient with specific wound drains and asked which benefit was being targeted, wound drainage could be a defensible answer — but in the context of post-abdominal surgery and a patient who is questioning the position, respiratory support is always the priority rationale.*

### Question 5 of 50 — Restraint Use

**Topic:** Patient Safety & Restraints | **Concept:** TJC Restraint Guidelines; Proper Application

**Question:** The Joint Commission issues guidelines regarding use of restraints. In which case is a restraint properly used?

|---|---|---|
| 1 | A client crying quietly: "I don't feel like life is worth living anymore." | ❌ NO |
| 2 | A client reporting increasing shortness of breath when walking to the bathroom | ✅ YES |
| 3 | A client who has not had a bowel movement in three days requesting a laxative | ❌ NO |
| 4 | A client expressing concern about missing their child's school recital | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 2**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** A client expressing suicidal ideation ("I don't feel like life is worth living anymore") represents an urgent **safety** need — Level 2 of Maslow's hierarchy. ❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** Constipation and the request for a laxative represent a physiological need (Level 1 — elimination) that is real but not urgent or life-threatening. Three days without a bowel movement in a hospitalized adult is a common finding that warrants attention, but it poses no immediate threat to life or safety. Under Maslow's framework, this physiological need is addressed after more immediately threatening physiological and safety concerns. *Note: The timing of this question is important — the client has been constipated for three days and is requesting a laxative, indicating tolerance of the situation. There is no escalating or acute finding here.*

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** A client worried about missing their child's school recital is expressing a **love and belonging** need (Level 3 of Maslow's hierarchy). This is a valid emotional concern that deserves acknowledgment and supportive communication, but it is the lowest-priority need among the four clients presented. Higher-level needs are only addressed after lower-level (more fundamental) needs are met.

### Question 9 of 50 — Post-Operative Pain Assessment

**Topic:** Pain Management | **Concept:** Pain Assessment; Nursing Process; Assessment Before Intervention

**Question:** A patient returning after knee surgery verbalizes pain at the surgical site. The nurse's first action is to:

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Call the patient's health care provider | ❌ NO |
| 2 | Administer pain medication as ordered | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Check the patient's vital signs | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Assess the characteristics of the pain | ✅ YES |

**Correct answer: 4**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** Calling the healthcare provider before assessing the patient is premature and skips the assessment phase of the nursing process entirely. The nurse must first gather objective data about the pain — its characteristics, severity, location, and any associated findings — before reporting to a provider. Calling without this information does not allow for effective communication (e.g., using SBAR) and may result in an incomplete or inaccurate report.

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** Administering pain medication before assessing the nature of the pain bypasses the assessment phase and could be unsafe. ✅ **Option 4 — Correct:** The nursing process begins with **assessment**. When a patient reports pain, the nurse must first characterize the pain fully using the PQRST framework — determine the location, quality, severity (0–10), what makes it better or worse, and its timing. This information is essential for determining whether the pain is expected post-operative pain, a sign of a complication, or something requiring immediate escalation. Assessment always precedes diagnosis, planning, and intervention.

### Question 10 of 50 — Diabetic Neuropathy: Foot Care & Skin Integrity

**Topic:** Hygiene & Skin Care | **Concept:** Diabetic Foot Care; Peripheral Neuropathy; Injury Prevention

**Question:** A client with diabetic neuropathy has decreased sensation in both feet. Which intervention is most appropriate?

| # | Intervention | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Encourage the client to walk barefoot in their room to improve circulation | ❌ NO |
| 2 | Apply heat packs to the client's feet to promote vasodilation | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Assess the feet daily and encourage the client to wear cotton socks and supportive footwear | ✅ YES |
| 4 | Use adhesive bandages for minor cuts and instruct the client to monitor for drainage | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 3**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** Walking barefoot is dangerous for any patient with peripheral neuropathy. Pain normally signals injury and prompts protective action — when this system is impaired, a client may step on sharp objects, sustain thermal burns, or develop blisters without realizing it. Even walking on smooth indoor floors creates friction and pressure. *Note: In healthy individuals without neuropathy, walking barefoot indoors may be acceptable — the critical issue here is that this client cannot detect injury through sensation.*

Together, these measures form the foundation of evidence-based diabetic foot care.

Instructing the client to "monitor for drainage" places the burden of detection on a patient whose sensation is impaired — they may not notice signs of infection until the wound is severely compromised. This approach is reactive and insufficient. Comprehensive daily inspection by the nurse and consistent protective practices are required. *Note: If a minor wound were already present, appropriate wound care would be indicated — but the primary teaching priority is prevention, not reactive monitoring.*

### Question 11 of 50 — Restorative Sleep Interventions

**Topic:** Sleep | **Concept:** Sleep Promotion; Hospitalized Older Adults; Evidence-Based Interventions

**Question:** Which actions are appropriate to promote restorative sleep for a hospitalized older adult? *(Select all that apply)*

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Offer a back massage before bedtime | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Encourage the client to watch television until falling asleep | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Reduce environmental noise and dim the lights in the room | ✅ YES |
| 4 | Avoid administering diuretics late in the evening | ✅ YES |
| 5 | Ensure the client's pain is managed before bedtime | ✅ YES |
| 6 | Wake the client every 2 hours to assess orientation and skin integrity | ❌ NO |

**Correct answers: 1, 3, 4, 5**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** A back massage before bedtime promotes relaxation by stimulating the parasympathetic nervous system, reducing muscle tension, and facilitating the transition to sleep. It is a well-supported non-pharmacological nursing intervention for sleep promotion and is particularly beneficial for hospitalized patients who may have difficulty relaxing in an unfamiliar environment.

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** Encouraging television use until the client falls asleep is counterproductive. The blue light emitted by screens suppresses melatonin production, and the stimulating content of television keeps the brain cognitively active. Both of these effects delay sleep onset. Evidence consistently shows that screen time near bedtime disrupts sleep quality. *Note: This is a commonly held misconception — many people believe TV helps them fall asleep, when in reality it delays and fragments sleep.*

✅ **Option 3 — Correct:** The hospital environment is a major disruptor of sleep — noise from alarms, staff conversations, and equipment, combined with artificial lighting, suppresses the natural sleep-wake cycle. Reducing environmental noise and dimming lights in the evening helps restore the environmental cues that signal the body to prepare for sleep (circadian rhythm support). This is one of the most impactful nursing interventions for improving hospital sleep quality.

✅ **Option 4 — Correct:** Diuretics increase urine output. When administered late in the evening, they cause nocturia — the client must awaken to void, fragmenting sleep. Scheduling diuretics earlier in the day (typically morning or early afternoon) reduces nighttime voiding episodes without compromising the therapeutic effect of the medication.

✅ **Option 5 — Correct:** Unmanaged pain is one of the most common and significant barriers to sleep in hospitalized patients. Pain activates the sympathetic nervous system and makes it physically impossible to relax into deep sleep. Proactively assessing and managing pain before bedtime — rather than waiting for the patient to request medication — is essential for promoting sleep.

❌ **Option 6 — Incorrect:** Waking a client every 2 hours for orientation checks and skin integrity assessment is a clinical intervention for specific high-risk patients (e.g., patients with altered mental status or high pressure injury risk), not a routine sleep promotion strategy. Frequent nighttime awakenings are themselves a major cause of non-restorative sleep in hospitalized patients. If a patient requires 2-hourly assessments for clinical reasons, nursing staff should cluster these checks efficiently to minimize disruption, not add them as a sleep promotion measure.

### Question 12 of 50 — Wound Healing: Stage IV Pressure Ulcer

**Topic:** Skin Integrity & Wound Care | **Concept:** Wound Healing Intention; Pressure Ulcer Staging

**Question:** A patient has a Stage IV pressure ulcer. Which type of healing will the nurse consider when planning care?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Partial-thickness wound repair | ❌ NO |
| 2 | Full-thickness wound repair | ✅ YES |
| 3 | Primary intention | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Tertiary intention | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 2**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** Partial-thickness wound repair applies to injuries that involve only the epidermis and/or the superficial dermis. These wounds heal primarily through epithelial cell migration from the wound edges and remaining hair follicles or sweat glands. Stage I and Stage II pressure ulcers may heal by partial-thickness repair. A Stage IV ulcer, by definition, involves full-thickness tissue loss with exposure of bone, tendon, or muscle — far beyond the superficial layers involved in partial-thickness repair.

✅ **Option 2 — Correct:** A Stage IV pressure ulcer involves full-thickness tissue loss — extending through the dermis, subcutaneous tissue, and exposing deep structures such as bone, tendon, or muscle. These wounds heal by **secondary intention**: the wound fills from the base upward with granulation tissue, then contracts and re-epithelializes. This is a slow process involving significant tissue regeneration, requires meticulous wound management, and results in substantial scar formation. Full-thickness wound repair is the correct framework for planning care.

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** Primary intention healing requires wound edges to be clean, approximated (brought together), and closed — typically with sutures, staples, or surgical adhesive — with minimal tissue loss. This results in a narrow, clean scar. A Stage IV pressure ulcer has extensive tissue loss and an open wound bed that cannot be approximated. Primary intention is the healing method for surgical incisions and clean lacerations, not pressure ulcers. *Note: Primary intention is the correct answer if the question were about wound healing in a post-surgical patient (see Question 43).*

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** Tertiary intention (delayed primary closure) involves intentionally leaving a wound open initially — typically because it is contaminated or infected — and then surgically closing it at a later date once the risk of infection has been controlled. This approach is used for certain traumatic wounds, abscesses, and contaminated surgical sites, not for chronic pressure ulcers. Pressure ulcers are managed as open wounds healing by secondary intention.

### Question 14 of 50 — Nasopharyngeal Suctioning

| 1 | Perform at least four suctioning passes but no more than five | ❌ NO |

### Question 15 of 50 — Tympanic Temperature in a Child

**Topic:** Vital Signs | **Concept:** Tympanic Temperature; Pediatric vs. Adult Technique

**Question:** The nurse is obtaining a tympanic temperature from a 4-year-old febrile child. Which action should the nurse take?

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Pull the pinna down and back before inserting the thermometer | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Place the probe loosely at the opening of the ear canal | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Gently pull the pinna up and back before inserting | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Insert the thermometer probe deeply into the ear canal | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 1**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** In young children (typically under 3 years of age, with the technique often extended to children up to school age in clinical practice), the ear canal curves **downward and forward**. Pulling the pinna **down and back** straightens this curve, allowing the infrared sensor to have a direct line of sight to the tympanic membrane and obtain an accurate reading. This is the established pediatric technique and reflects the anatomical difference in ear canal orientation between young children and adults.

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** A loose probe seal at the ear canal opening will allow ambient air and environmental infrared radiation to enter the sensor, producing an inaccurate (usually falsely low) reading. ❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** Pulling the pinna **up and back** is the correct technique for **adults and older children** (≥3 years). In adults, the ear canal curves upward and backward, so elevating the pinna straightens it. Applying this technique to a young child would actually increase the curvature of the canal, blocking the sensor's view of the tympanic membrane and producing an inaccurate temperature. *Note: This would be the correct answer if the patient were an adult.*

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** The tympanic thermometer probe should never be inserted deeply into the ear canal. The device is designed to take a contactless infrared reading from the entrance of the canal — it does not need to physically touch the tympanic membrane. Deep insertion risks perforating the tympanic membrane, causing pain, and damaging sensitive ear structures. The probe seals at the opening of the canal, not inside it.

### Question 16 of 50 — Cane Use with Hemiparesis

**Topic:** Activity, Immobility & Patient Positioning | **Concept:** Assistive Devices; Cane Use; Hemiparesis

**Question:** A nurse is teaching a client with hemiparesis to use a cane. Which should the nurse teach?

| # | Instruction | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Move forward 1 step with the weak leg first, followed by the strong leg and cane | ❌ NO |
| 2 | Adjust the cane height 12 inches lower than the waist | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Hold the cane in the strong hand when walking | ✅ YES |
| 4 | Lean over onto the cane when walking | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 3**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** The correct gait sequence with a cane for a patient with hemiparesis is: advance the **cane and weak leg simultaneously**, then bring the strong leg forward. Moving the weak leg alone first — without the simultaneous support of the cane — leaves the patient without lateral stability on their weakened side during weight transfer. *Note: The correct sequence is cane + weak leg together, then strong leg.*

This placement widens the base of support and transfers load away from the weakened lower extremity during gait. Holding the cane on the weak side would actually reduce stability.

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** Leaning heavily onto the cane shifts the patient's center of gravity forward and over the cane, increasing fall risk. The cane provides lateral support and assistive balance — not a fulcrum to lean on. Excessive forward lean also increases mechanical stress on the wrist, elbow, and shoulder. The patient should maintain an upright posture with only slight weight-bearing through the cane hand.

### Question 17 of 50 — Subjective vs. Objective Data

|---|---|---|
| 1 | Patient temperature | ❌ NO |
| 2 | Patient wound appearance | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Patient describing excitement about discharge | ✅ YES |
| 4 | Patient pacing the floor awaiting test results | ❌ NO |
| 5 | Patient reports fear regarding upcoming surgery | ✅ YES |

### Question 18 of 50 — Droplet Precautions: Influenza

**Topic:** Infection Prevention and Control | **Concept:** Transmission-Based Precautions; Droplet Precautions

**Question:** A nurse is caring for a client with influenza. Which actions should the nurse include when implementing droplet precautions? *(Select all that apply)*

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Place client in a private room or cohort with a client with the same infection | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Wear a surgical mask when within 3 feet of the client | ✅ YES |
| 3 | Use airborne isolation room with negative pressure ventilation | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Wear gloves when touching the client or contaminated items | ✅ YES |
| 5 | Instruct the client to wear a mask when outside the room | ✅ YES |
| 6 | Wash hands before and after client contact | ✅ YES |

**Correct answers: 1, 2, 4, 5, 6**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** Isolation in a private room limits the exposure of other patients to large respiratory droplets, which travel up to 3 feet and land on mucous membranes. If a private room is unavailable, cohorting (placing the patient with another patient infected with the same organism) is acceptable. This is a required component of droplet precautions.

✅ **Option 2 — Correct:** Influenza is transmitted via large respiratory droplets produced by coughing, sneezing, or talking. These droplets fall quickly and do not remain airborne, but they can land on the mucous membranes of anyone within approximately 3 feet. A surgical mask worn within this range provides an effective barrier. The surgical mask does not need to be worn when the nurse is more than 3 feet away from the patient with the door closed.

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** Negative-pressure airborne isolation rooms are reserved for pathogens transmitted via **airborne** droplet nuclei (≤5 microns) that remain suspended in the air for prolonged periods and can travel long distances. These include tuberculosis (TB), measles, and varicella. Influenza is a **droplet** pathogen — its larger particles fall to the ground quickly and do not require the sustained air exchange of a negative-pressure room. Using a negative-pressure room for influenza wastes a scarce resource. *Note: If the question described TB, measles, or varicella, a negative-pressure room would be the correct answer.*

✅ **Option 4 — Correct:** Gloves are a component of standard precautions and are required when touching the patient or any contaminated surfaces or items. Contact with respiratory secretions, contaminated bed linens, or used tissues requires gloves. Gloves are worn in combination with the surgical mask for droplet precautions.

✅ **Option 5 — Correct:** When the patient with influenza must leave the room for procedures or transport, they should wear a surgical mask to protect other patients and staff in the hallway and common areas. This is an important containment measure to prevent inadvertent transmission outside the isolation room.

✅ **Option 6 — Correct:** Hand hygiene before and after every patient contact is a universal standard precaution that applies in all patient care situations, including those under transmission-based precautions. It is the single most effective measure for preventing pathogen spread.

### Question 19 of 50 — Crutch Fitting Assessment

**Topic:** Activity, Immobility & Patient Positioning | **Concept:** Crutch Fitting; Correct Technique

**Question:** After educating a patient on crutch adjustment, which findings demonstrate correct fitting? *(Select all that apply)*

| # | Finding | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | The hand grips of the crutches are even with the mid-forearm | ❌ NO |
| 2 | When the patient grips the hand grips, the elbow bends at about 30 degrees | ✅ YES |
| 3 | The patient has a 2–3 finger width distance between the axillae and crutch rest pad | ✅ YES |
| 4 | The patient places weight on the axillae rather than the hands while ambulating | ❌ NO |

**Correct answers: 2, 3**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** Hand grips should be positioned at the level of the **greater trochanter** — the bony prominence of the hip — not at mid-forearm. When standing upright with arms relaxed at the sides, the hand grip should align with the wrist crease. Mid-forearm placement would result in the elbow being bent too acutely, reducing the mechanical efficiency of weight-bearing and creating abnormal stress on the wrist and shoulder.

✅ **Option 2 — Correct:** When the patient grips the hand grips while standing, the elbows should flex at approximately **15–30°** (some sources cite 20–30°). This angle provides optimal biomechanical leverage for pushing down on the grips and propelling the body forward, while also absorbing impact forces. If the elbows are fully extended or excessively bent, the crutch height is incorrect.

✅ **Option 3 — Correct:** A **2–3 finger width** gap between the axillary pad and the patient's armpit confirms that the crutches are the correct height. Resting body weight on the axillary pads is the most dangerous crutch-use error because it compresses the brachial plexus, which can cause temporary or permanent nerve damage (crutch palsy). The axillary pads serve only as stabilizers — all downward force during ambulation must go through the hands. Patients who fatigue and slump onto the axillary pads are at significant risk. *Note: This is the most clinically important crutch-use concept — it frequently appears on nursing exams because the injury is both preventable and significant.*

### Question 21 of 50 — Oxygen Therapy Safety

**Topic:** Oxygenation | **Concept:** Oxygen Safety in the Hospital Setting

**Question:** Which actions should the nurse take to ensure oxygen safety? *(Select all that apply)*

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Remove all flammable materials (petroleum-based lotions) from the client's vicinity | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Place "No Smoking" signs clearly in the client's room | ✅ YES |
| 3 | Ensure electrical equipment near the client is properly grounded | ✅ YES |
| 4 | Use cotton bedding over synthetic materials | ✅ YES |
| 5 | Allow the client to adjust the oxygen flow rate as needed for comfort | ❌ NO |
| 6 | Keep oxygen tanks secured in an upright position at all times | ✅ YES |

**Correct answers: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** Oxygen dramatically accelerates combustion — materials that are mildly flammable in room air can ignite explosively in an oxygen-enriched environment. Petroleum-based products (Vaseline, mineral oil, petroleum-based lotions or lip balms) are particularly dangerous because they are both flammable and commonly found near patients' faces where oxygen is delivered. All such materials must be removed from the vicinity and replaced with water-soluble alternatives.

✅ **Option 2 — Correct:** Smoking introduces an open flame or spark in an environment with elevated oxygen concentration — a potentially fatal combination. "No Smoking" signs must be posted at all entrances to a room where oxygen is in use, including home oxygen environments. This applies to visitors, family members, and the patient themselves.

✅ **Option 3 — Correct:** Improperly grounded electrical equipment can generate sparks. In an oxygen-enriched environment, even a small spark can ignite flammable materials. All electrical equipment used near oxygen delivery systems must be hospital-grade, inspected, and properly grounded. Frayed cords or equipment showing signs of malfunction must be removed from service.

✅ **Option 4 — Correct:** Synthetic materials (polyester, nylon) can generate static electricity through friction — particularly from bed linens and patient gowns. Static discharge can produce a spark. Cotton bedding is preferred because it generates significantly less static electricity. This is especially relevant in low-humidity environments where static buildup is more pronounced.

❌ **Option 5 — Incorrect:** Oxygen is a prescribed medication — its flow rate is set by the healthcare provider based on the patient's clinical assessment and oxygenation needs. Allowing patients to independently adjust their oxygen flow rate is unsafe. Too little oxygen could cause hypoxemia; too much oxygen can be harmful in patients with conditions such as COPD (hypercapnic respiratory drive), and flow rate adjustments should only be made by clinical staff based on SpO₂ monitoring and provider orders.

✅ **Option 6 — Correct:** Oxygen tanks must always be stored and used in the upright position and secured to prevent tipping or rolling. If a tank falls, the valve at the top can shear off, turning the cylinder into a high-velocity projectile. Tanks should be chained or strapped to a wall or cart. When not in use, they must be stored away from heat, open flames, and electrical equipment.

### Question 22 of 50 — CPAP Teaching: OSA

**Topic:** Oxygenation | **Concept:** CPAP; OSA; Patient Teaching; Identifying Incorrect Understanding

**Question:** Which statement by the client indicates a need for further teaching about CPAP?

| # | Statement | Needs Teaching? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "I should wear the CPAP mask every night while I sleep." | ❌ NO — correct understanding |
| 2 | "This machine helps keep my airway open so I can breathe better at night." | ❌ NO — correct understanding |
| 3 | "If I feel better after a few nights, I can stop using the machine." | ✅ YES — incorrect; needs teaching |
| 4 | "I'll clean the mask and tubing regularly to prevent infection." | ❌ NO — correct understanding |

**Correct answer: 3** *(the statement that requires further teaching)*

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Correct understanding (no teaching needed):** Daily, every-night use of CPAP while sleeping is exactly what is required. CPAP is only effective when worn consistently. Intermittent use — skipping nights, removing the mask during the night — allows apneic episodes to recur and eliminates the therapeutic benefit. This statement reflects correct understanding.

❌ **Option 2 — Correct understanding (no teaching needed):** This accurately describes the mechanism of CPAP therapy. Obstructive sleep apnea occurs because the soft tissues of the upper airway collapse during sleep, blocking airflow. CPAP delivers a continuous stream of pressurized air that acts as a pneumatic splint, keeping the airway open throughout sleep. This statement reflects accurate patient understanding.

This statement reflects correct understanding.

### Question 23 of 50 — Good Samaritan Law

**Topic:** Legal & Ethical Concepts | **Concept:** Good Samaritan Law; Protected vs. Non-Protected Actions

**Question:** A nurse witnesses a motor vehicle accident and stops to assist. Which action is protected under the Good Samaritan Law?

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Performing emergency CPR even though the nurse is off duty | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Providing detailed medical advice over the phone to the victim's family | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Transporting the injured driver to the hospital in their personal vehicle | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Leaving the scene after ensuring the victim is stable and EMS is on the way | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 1**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** Performing CPR at an accident scene while off duty is the classic example of an action protected by Good Samaritan legislation. Good Samaritan laws exist in every U.S. state and are designed to encourage bystanders with medical skills to provide emergency care without fear of civil liability. Protection applies when the care is: provided voluntarily (not as part of a paid professional role), rendered in good faith (reasonable attempt to help), at the scene of an emergency, and without gross negligence or recklessness. An off-duty nurse performing CPR at an accident scene meets all of these criteria.

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** Providing detailed medical advice by telephone is not considered "on-scene emergency care" — it is remote consultation. Good Samaritan laws specifically protect emergency care rendered **in person at the scene**. Telephone advice is subject to different legal standards, and a healthcare professional who gives phone guidance could be establishing a professional duty that creates liability. Additionally, advice given without direct patient assessment is inherently incomplete and carries its own risks.

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** Transporting an injured victim in a personal vehicle is not protected by Good Samaritan laws and introduces significant liability. Emergency medical care during transport requires appropriate equipment, training, and personnel — none of which are available in a personal vehicle. An injury that worsens during unauthorized transport could result in legal action against the transporting individual. EMS transport is always preferred. The nurse's protected role is to provide immediate on-scene care and await EMS arrival.

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** While leaving the scene after ensuring EMS is on the way may be practically reasonable, it is not specifically the action that is **protected** under Good Samaritan law — the protection applies to the emergency care rendered, not the act of departing. Additionally, if the victim's condition deteriorates between the nurse's departure and EMS arrival, questions about duty and abandonment could arise in some jurisdictions. The most clearly protected action is the emergency care itself — option 1.

| 2 | Decrease the number of repetitions performed | ✅ YES |

**Rationales:**

This option is a distractor designed to test whether students can distinguish between a meaningful patient report and a nursing workaround.

It places the nurse's care plan goals above the patient's expressed experience and autonomy. Forcing or coercing a patient to continue an exercise they have stated they cannot tolerate is inappropriate, potentially harmful, and disrespectful of patient-centered care principles. Fatigue during activity in a post-stroke patient may also signal a meaningful clinical change that requires assessment.

✅ **Option 6 — Correct:** Fatigue during mild physical activity such as ROM exercises may indicate more than simple tiredness — it could be a sign of cardiac compromise (reduced cardiac output post-stroke), respiratory impairment, anemia, medication effects, or neurological changes. Assessment always accompanies any unexpected patient response.

### Question 25 of 50 — Heparin Subcutaneous Injection

**Topic:** Medication Administration | **Concept:** Heparin Subcutaneous Injection Technique; Patient Education; Identifying Errors

**Question:** While observing the patient self-administer Heparin, which action requires re-education?

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | The patient injects the needle into the fatty tissue of the abdomen | ❌ NO — correct technique |
| 2 | The patient injects the needle 1 inch away from the umbilicus | ✅ YES — requires re-education |
| 3 | The patient rotates the injection site | ❌ NO — correct technique |
| 4 | The patient does not massage the injection site after administering the medication | ❌ NO — correct technique (do NOT massage) |

**Correct answer: 2**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Correct technique (no re-education needed):** The fatty subcutaneous tissue of the abdomen is the preferred and recommended injection site for subcutaneous heparin. The abdominal tissue provides a large, accessible subcutaneous fat pad, and heparin is reliably absorbed from this site. The abdomen is preferred over the thigh or upper arm for subcutaneous anticoagulants because of its consistent absorption profile.

✅ **Option 2 — Incorrect technique (requires re-education):** The injection must be administered **at least 2 inches (5 cm)** from the umbilicus, not 1 inch. ❌ **Option 3 — Correct technique (no re-education needed):** Site rotation is essential for subcutaneous heparin. Repeated injections into the same site cause lipodystrophy (fatty tissue breakdown or thickening), which alters drug absorption and can lead to unpredictable anticoagulation. A systematic rotation pattern — moving through designated abdominal quadrants or zones — ensures that no single area receives injections too frequently.

❌ **Option 4 — Correct technique (no re-education needed):** NOT massaging the injection site is correct. This is one of the most commonly tested points for heparin administration. Massaging the site after injection disperses the medication too rapidly into the surrounding tissue and capillaries, breaking the protective subcutaneous depot that allows gradual, controlled absorption. Rapid dispersal increases bruising (hematoma formation) and can alter the anticoagulant effect. Patients must be clearly instructed never to massage after a heparin injection. *Note: This is the most commonly tested heparin error — if the question presented "patient massages the injection site" as an option, that would be the action requiring re-education.*

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** Urine stored at room temperature undergoes rapid bacterial multiplication. Bacteria naturally present in the sample proliferate, altering the white blood cell count, bacterial culture results, and chemical composition of the specimen. If a urine specimen cannot be delivered to the laboratory within **30 minutes** of collection, it must be **refrigerated or placed on ice** to slow bacterial growth and preserve specimen integrity. A specimen stored at room temperature for several hours will produce unreliable and potentially misleading results.

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** The urine collection bag (receptacle) of an indwelling catheter is **not sterile**. Urine accumulates in the bag over time, bacteria can proliferate even in a closed system, and the bag itself is not maintained under sterile conditions. Collecting from the drainage bag produces a contaminated specimen that cannot be used for a meaningful culture result. All sterile urine specimens from an indwelling catheter must be collected from the **specimen port** on the drainage tubing — not from the bag.

✅ **Option 4 — Correct:** Aspirating approximately 3–5 mL of urine from the **specimen port** (the self-sealing rubber port on the drainage tubing) using a sterile syringe and needle is the correct and only acceptable method for obtaining a sterile urine specimen from an indwelling catheter. This technique maintains the closed system integrity, obtains fresh urine from the tubing before it reaches the bag, and produces a specimen with minimal contamination. The port is cleaned with an antiseptic wipe before aspiration.

✅ **Option 5 — Correct:** The first morning void is discarded at the start of a 24-hour urine collection because it contains urine that accumulated in the bladder overnight — urine produced before the designated collection period began. Discarding this initial void establishes the precise starting point of the 24-hour period. All subsequent urine — including the final void 24 hours later — is collected and refrigerated. This ensures that exactly 24 hours of urine production is represented in the sample.

| # | Question | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | What does the pain feel like? (sharp, dull, burning) | ❌ NO |
| 2 | What makes the pain better or worse? | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Where is the pain located, and does it radiate? | ❌ NO |
| 4 | On a scale of 0 to 10, how severe is your pain? | ✅ YES |

**Correct answer: 4**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** "What does the pain feel like?" — asking whether it is sharp, dull, burning, aching, throbbing, or stabbing — corresponds to **Q = Quality**. Quality describes the sensory character of the pain. This is an important component of pain assessment because different qualities suggest different pathophysiology (e.g., burning pain suggests neuropathic involvement; pressure or squeezing suggests cardiac or visceral origin).

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** "What makes the pain better or worse?" corresponds to **P = Provocation/Palliation**. ✅ **Option 4 — Correct:** "On a scale of 0 to 10, how severe is your pain?" corresponds to **S = Severity**. The numeric rating scale (0 = no pain, 10 = worst pain imaginable) provides a standardized, quantifiable measure of pain intensity that can be compared across time to evaluate treatment effectiveness. Severity assessment is required before and after any pain intervention.

### Question 34 of 50 — Types of Exercise: Isotonic

**Topic:** Activity, Immobility & Patient Positioning | **Concept:** Types of Exercise; Isotonic vs. Isometric vs. Resistive Isometric

**Question:** Isotonic, isometric, and resistive isometric are categories of exercise. Which exercises are considered isotonic?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Bicycling, swimming, walking, jogging, dancing | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Tightening or tensing muscles without moving body parts | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Push-ups, hip lifting, pushing feet against a footboard | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Quadriceps set exercises and contraction of gluteal muscles | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 1**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** Isotonic exercises involve **muscle contraction with movement** — the muscle changes in tension, the joint moves through a range of motion, and muscle length changes (shortens or lengthens). These exercises are dynamic and functional. The examples given — bicycling, swimming, walking, jogging, and dancing — all involve rhythmic, repetitive muscle contractions through full ranges of motion. Isotonic exercise is the foundation of cardiovascular conditioning and functional strength rehabilitation.

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** Tightening or tensing muscles without moving body parts describes **isometric exercise**. ❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** Push-ups, hip lifts, and pushing the feet against a footboard are examples of **resistive isometric** exercise — the muscle contracts against an external resistance but no joint movement occurs (or minimal movement). These exercises build strength through resistance loading without range of motion. Resistive isometrics are used when joint stability is required or during early rehabilitation when ROM is restricted. *Note: This would be the correct answer if the question asked about resistive isometric exercises.*

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** Quadriceps sets (tightening the quad muscle while the leg is straight) and gluteal contractions (squeezing the buttocks) are **isometric exercises** — no joint movement occurs; only muscle tension changes. These exercises are commonly prescribed post-operatively (e.g., after knee replacement) to maintain muscle tone and prevent atrophy while range of motion is restricted. *Note: These are isometric, same category as option 2.*

### Question 36 of 50 — Blood Pressure: Accurate Measurement

**Topic:** Vital Signs | **Concept:** Blood Pressure Measurement; Avoiding Measurement Error

**Question:** The nurse ensures accurate BP measurement by avoiding which of the following?

| # | Action | Avoid? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Seating the client with arm bared, supported, and at heart level | ❌ NO — correct technique |
| 2 | Measuring BP after the client has been seated quietly for 5 minutes | ❌ NO — correct technique |
| 3 | Using a cuff with a rubber bladder that encircles at least 80% of the limb | ❌ NO — correct technique |
| 4 | Taking a BP within 15 minutes after nicotine or caffeine ingestion | ✅ YES — this is the error to avoid |

**Correct answer: 4**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Correct technique (should NOT be avoided):** Seating the client with the arm bared, supported at the level of the heart is standard BP measurement protocol. The arm must be at heart level because holding the arm above or below that level introduces hydrostatic pressure errors — arm below heart level produces falsely elevated readings; arm above heart level produces falsely low readings. Clothing constricting the arm (a rolled-up sleeve, for instance) can also artifactually elevate readings.

❌ **Option 2 — Correct technique (should NOT be avoided):** Having the client sit quietly for at least 5 minutes before measurement allows the cardiovascular system to reach a resting steady state. Physical activity, walking, anxiety, and even talking immediately before BP measurement can transiently elevate the reading. The 5-minute rest period is a standard evidence-based recommendation for accurate resting blood pressure assessment.

❌ **Option 3 — Correct technique (should NOT be avoided):** The cuff bladder should encircle at least 80% of the limb circumference. Using a cuff that is too small for the patient's arm (such as using a standard adult cuff on an obese patient) produces falsely elevated readings because the cuff must be inflated to a higher pressure to compress a larger limb. Using a correctly sized cuff is essential for accuracy.

✅ **Option 4 — This IS the error to avoid:** Taking a blood pressure within **30 minutes** of caffeine or nicotine ingestion is contraindicated — not 15 minutes. Both caffeine and nicotine are vasoconstrictors and stimulants that transiently elevate blood pressure and heart rate. Measuring within 15 minutes (as this option states) is insufficient — the standard interval is 30 minutes to allow these effects to dissipate and obtain a true resting measurement. This is a frequently tested distinction and a common clinical error.

### Question 41 of 50 — Stage III Pressure Ulcer Findings

| 3 | Partial-thickness loss of dermis as a shallow open ulcer with red-pink wound bed | ❌ NO |

| 5 | Full-thickness tissue loss exposing bone, tendon, or muscle | ❌ NO |

❌ **Option 5 — Incorrect:** Full-thickness tissue loss with **exposure of bone, tendon, or muscle** defines a **Stage IV** pressure injury. This is the most severe staged category. ❌ **Option 6 — Incorrect:** A blister (intact or ruptured) involving the epidermis and dermis defines a **Stage II** pressure injury. ### Question 42 of 50 — Ear Drop Administration: Adult

**Topic:** Medication Administration | **Concept:** Ear Drop Administration; Adult vs. Pediatric Technique

**Question:** The nurse is administering ear drops to an adult client with external otitis. Which action demonstrates correct technique?

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Pull the pinna down and back | ❌ NO |
| 2 | Keep the client in a side-lying position with the affected ear up for 2–3 minutes after administration | ✅ YES |
| 3 | Hold the ear drop bottle in the refrigerator until administration to preserve potency | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Insert a cotton-tipped applicator to push the medication deeper into the ear canal | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 2**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** Pulling the pinna **down and back** is the correct technique for **children under 3 years of age** — it straightens the downward-curving pediatric ear canal to allow better visualization and drop delivery. In **adults**, the ear canal curves upward and backward — the correct technique is to pull the pinna **up and back**. Applying the pediatric technique to an adult would worsen the canal curvature and reduce medication penetration to the affected area. *Note: This would be the correct answer if the patient were a young child (under 3 years).*

If the client sits up immediately, the drops will simply run back out.

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** Ear drops should be warmed to **room temperature** before administration — not kept refrigerated until use. Cold ear drops cause **thermal stimulation of the vestibular system**, which produces a reflex response of sudden dizziness, nausea, and vertigo (caloric response). This is uncomfortable for the patient and can be disorienting. Warming the drops by holding the bottle in the hand for a few minutes before administration is the standard approach. Refrigeration may be appropriate for long-term storage per manufacturer instructions, but the drops must be warmed before use.

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** Inserting a cotton-tipped applicator (Q-tip) into the ear canal is contraindicated. It risks injuring the tympanic membrane or ear canal, can push wax and debris deeper, and provides no benefit for medication delivery. The drops are instilled as a liquid — they travel through the canal by gravity. Cotton wicks or loose cotton balls are sometimes placed gently at the outer opening of the canal to prevent drops from running out, but cotton-tipped applicators should never be inserted into the canal.

### Question 43 of 50 — Wound Healing by Primary Intention

**Topic:** Skin Integrity & Wound Care | **Concept:** Wound Healing; Primary vs. Secondary Intention

**Question:** Which wound will most likely heal by primary intention?

| # | Wound | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cut in the skin from a kitchen knife | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Excoriated perineal area | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Abrasion of the skin | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Pressure ulcer | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 1**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** A clean knife cut produces a wound with **smooth, approximated edges** and minimal surrounding tissue damage. The wound edges can be brought together (with sutures, staples, or skin closure strips if needed) so that healing can occur directly across a narrow line with minimal granulation tissue formation. This is the definition of **primary intention** healing — clean edges, minimal tissue loss, minimal scarring.

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** Excoriation refers to the loss of the superficial skin layers (epidermis and/or superficial dermis) due to friction, scratching, or moisture — typically involving an irregular area of skin rather than a clean-edged wound. Excoriated skin cannot be approximated (the wound edges are irregular and the tissue is fragile). This type of wound heals by **secondary intention** — through granulation tissue formation, wound contraction, and epithelial migration over the granulation tissue. *Note: Secondary intention is also the healing method for pressure ulcers, burns, and large open wounds.*

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** An abrasion involves the loss of superficial skin layers over a broad, irregular surface area — typically caused by friction against a rough surface. The wound has no clearly defined edges that can be approximated. Healing occurs by **secondary intention** (epithelial migration across the granulating surface). Abrasions heal without scarring if superficial, or with minimal scarring if deeper.

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** Pressure ulcers involve tissue loss over a bony prominence. They range from superficial (Stage I–II) to deep (Stage III–IV) and characteristically have irregular margins, significant tissue loss, and often necrotic tissue. The wound edges cannot be brought together surgically. Pressure ulcers heal by **secondary intention** — granulation tissue fills the wound bed from the base upward, followed by contraction and epithelialization. Stage IV pressure ulcers may take months to years to heal.

### Question 44 of 50 — Surgical Asepsis: Sterile Field

**Topic:** Infection Prevention and Control | **Concept:** Surgical Asepsis; Sterile Field Maintenance; Central Line Dressing Change

**Question:** Which actions indicate proper surgical asepsis when changing a central venous catheter dressing? *(Select all that apply)*

| # | Action | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Opening sterile packages away from the body | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Keeping the sterile field at or above waist level | ✅ YES |
| 3 | Touching the outer 1-inch border of the sterile field when arranging supplies | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Reaching over the sterile field only when wearing sterile gloves | ✅ YES |
| 5 | Turning the back to the sterile field briefly while retrieving supplies | ❌ NO |
| 6 | Donning sterile gloves before touching sterile dressings | ✅ YES |

**Correct answers: 1, 2, 4, 6**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** Opening sterile packages away from the body (and away from the sterile field) prevents the outer — non-sterile — packaging from contaminating the sterile field or the sterile contents. The package wrapping is not sterile; only the contents inside are. Opening toward the body risks dropping non-sterile packaging onto the field.

✅ **Option 2 — Correct:** Everything on or above waist level is considered within the sterile zone during surgical aseptic technique. Any item that drops below waist level — even momentarily — passes out of direct visualization and must be considered contaminated. This applies to sterile gloves, instruments, drapes, and all supplies.

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** The outer **1-inch border** of a sterile drape is considered **non-sterile**. This border contacts unsterile surfaces (the table edge, the draping process) during field setup. Touching this border — even with sterile gloves — contaminates the glove. All sterile supplies must be placed in the interior of the sterile field, well inside the 1-inch margin. *Note: A common error is assuming the entire drape is sterile — the border is always considered the non-sterile boundary.*

✅ **Option 4 — Correct:** Sterile gloves may cross over or reach into the sterile field because their outer surfaces are sterile. Only sterile-gloved hands may enter the sterile zone. However, even with sterile gloves, the nurse should avoid draping arms or sleeves over the field (non-sterile surfaces on the upper arm/torso could contaminate supplies below).

❌ **Option 5 — Incorrect:** Turning the back to the sterile field is a critical violation of surgical asepsis. A sterile field is considered contaminated the moment it passes out of the nurse's direct line of vision — because the nurse can no longer guarantee that nothing has fallen onto or contaminated the field. The nurse must always face the sterile field. If supplies need to be retrieved from behind or to the side, the entire field setup should be arranged to prevent this situation, or a second person should hand supplies to the nurse.

✅ **Option 6 — Correct:** Sterile gloves must be donned before touching any sterile dressings, supplies, or contents of sterile packages. Clean or ungloved hands are never placed on sterile items or within the sterile field. Donning sterile gloves using the closed or open glove technique (maintaining sterility of the glove exterior) is a prerequisite for the procedure.

### Question 45 of 50 — 24-Hour Urine Specimen Collection

**Topic:** Urinary Elimination | **Concept:** 24-Hour Urine Collection; Patient Teaching

**Question:** Which instructions should the nurse include when teaching a client to collect a 24-hour urine specimen? *(Select all that apply)*

| # | Instruction | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Begin by discarding the first morning urine, then collect all urine for the next 24 hours | ✅ YES |
| 2 | Keep the collection container refrigerated or on ice during the entire 24-hour period | ✅ YES |
| 3 | Void one last time at the end of the 24 hours and add this urine to the collection | ✅ YES |
| 4 | Collect urine only during waking hours; discard nighttime voids | ❌ NO |
| 5 | Avoid excessive physical activity during the 24-hour collection | ❌ NO |
| 6 | Notify the laboratory immediately if any urine is accidentally discarded | ✅ YES |

**Correct answers: 1, 2, 3, 6**

**Rationales:**

✅ **Option 1 — Correct:** The first morning void is discarded to establish the precise start time. This void contains urine that accumulated in the bladder from before the test began and would represent an unknown time period — including it would make the 24-hour total inaccurate. The clock starts when this first void is discarded.

✅ **Option 2 — Correct:** Urine stored at room temperature undergoes bacterial proliferation and chemical degradation. Refrigeration slows these processes and preserves the chemical composition and cellular components of the specimen. Some 24-hour collections require a preservative (acidic or basic, depending on the analyte) in the collection container — the nurse should verify laboratory instructions. Regardless, keeping the container cold is standard.

✅ **Option 3 — Correct:** The final void at the 24-hour mark (i.e., exactly 24 hours after the first void was discarded) is **included** in the collection. This void completes the 24-hour period and must be added to ensure all urine produced in the specified time window is captured. Omitting the final void makes the collection incomplete.

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** All urine produced during the entire 24-hour period must be collected — including **nighttime voids**. The purpose of a 24-hour collection is to measure the total daily production or excretion of a substance (e.g., creatinine, protein, cortisol). Omitting nighttime urine invalidates the test entirely. The patient must be instructed to keep a collection container accessible at the bedside overnight.

❌ **Option 5 — Incorrect:** Avoiding excessive physical activity is not a universal standard instruction for all 24-hour urine collections. Some specific tests (e.g., catecholamine collections for pheochromocytoma) may require activity restrictions because exercise can elevate catecholamine levels. However, this is not a blanket instruction for all 24-hour collections. Unless the laboratory or provider specifically instructs it, activity restriction is not required. *Note: Including this as a universal instruction would be incorrect teaching.*

✅ **Option 6 — Correct:** If any urine is accidentally discarded during the collection period — even a single void — the entire collection is potentially invalidated because the total cannot be accurately calculated. The laboratory must be notified immediately to determine whether the collection should be restarted or whether the partial collection can still be used for the specific test ordered. Instructing the patient to notify the nurse or laboratory in this situation is essential.

| 1 | "The client is a 72-year-old who had a total hip arthroplasty 24 hours ago." | ❌ NO |

### Question 48 of 50 — Legal Term: Nurse's Threatening Statement

**Topic:** Legal & Ethical Concepts | **Concept:** Legal Terms; Assault; Distinguishing Legal Concepts

**Question:** The nurse says, "If you keep ringing, there will come a time when I won't answer your bell." Which legal term applies?

| # | Term | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Slander | ❌ NO |
| 2 | Battery | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Assault | ✅ YES |
| 4 | Libel | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 3**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** Slander is a form of defamation — specifically, a **spoken** false statement that injures a person's reputation. This scenario does not involve a false statement about the patient's reputation — it is a threat about future behavior. Slander is not applicable here. *Note: Slander would be the correct term if a nurse verbally made false statements to coworkers about a patient's character or behavior.*

❌ **Option 2 — Incorrect:** Battery is the intentional **physical touching** of another person without their consent. It requires actual physical contact. ❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** Libel is a form of defamation involving a **written** false statement that damages a person's reputation. Like slander, it involves falsehood and reputational harm. ### Question 49 of 50 — Witnessing Informed Consent

**Topic:** Legal & Ethical Concepts | **Concept:** Informed Consent; Witness Role and Limitations

**Question:** When signing a form as a witness, your signature shows that the client:

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Is fully informed and is aware of all consequences | ❌ NO |
| 2 | Was awake, fully alert, and not medicated with narcotics | ❌ NO |
| 3 | Was free to sign without pressure | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Has signed that form and the witness saw it being done | ✅ YES |

**Correct answer: 4**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** Ensuring that the patient is fully informed about a procedure — including its risks, benefits, alternatives, and potential consequences — is the responsibility of the **provider** obtaining consent (typically the physician or advanced practice provider). The nurse who witnesses the signature does not verify the content or completeness of the informed consent conversation. If the nurse witnesses a signature but believes the patient has not been adequately informed, the nurse's role is to notify the provider — not to sign and assume the consent is valid.

### Question 50 of 50 — Pressure Ulcer: Failure to Heal

**Topic:** Skin Integrity & Wound Care | **Concept:** Wound Healing; Nutritional Factors; Protein

**Question:** A client on prolonged bed rest has a pressure ulcer showing no signs of healing despite skin care and turning every 2 hours. Which factor is most likely responsible?

| # | Factor | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Inadequate vitamin D intake | ❌ NO |
| 2 | Inadequate protein intake | ✅ YES |
| 3 | Inadequate massaging of the affected area | ❌ NO |
| 4 | Low calcium level | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: 2**

**Rationales:**

❌ **Option 1 — Incorrect:** Vitamin D plays a role in immune modulation, calcium metabolism, and has some effect on cell proliferation and wound healing. However, it is not the **primary** or most significant nutritional factor in pressure ulcer healing. Vitamin D deficiency can impair immunity and theoretically slow wound healing, but it does not have the central, rate-limiting role in tissue repair that protein does. Vitamin C is a more directly relevant micronutrient for wound healing (it is required for collagen synthesis), but even it is secondary to adequate protein intake.

✅ **Option 2 — Correct:** **Protein** is the most critical macronutrient for wound healing. Protein is required for:
- **Collagen synthesis** — the structural protein that forms the scaffold of new tissue
- **Immune function** — antibody production and neutrophil/macrophage activity to fight infection
- **Cell proliferation** — production of new epithelial cells, fibroblasts, and other healing cells
- **Maintaining oncotic pressure** — preventing edema in the wound bed that impairs healing

When protein intake is inadequate, none of these processes can proceed effectively. The wound stalls in the inflammatory phase, granulation tissue does not form, and the patient remains at ongoing risk for infection. Even with optimal positioning, turning schedules, and wound care, a protein-deficient patient's wound will not heal. This is the most common and most clinically significant nutritional barrier to pressure ulcer healing.

❌ **Option 3 — Incorrect:** Massaging the affected area over a pressure injury is not only unhelpful — it is **contraindicated**. Massage over a pressure injury or areas of non-blanchable redness causes additional mechanical trauma to already-damaged tissue, disrupts the fragile capillaries and newly forming granulation tissue, and accelerates tissue breakdown. This is a critical safety teaching point — patients and families sometimes believe massage will "improve circulation," but it causes harm. The correct intervention is **offloading pressure** (turning, repositioning, pressure-redistributing surfaces), not massage.

❌ **Option 4 — Incorrect:** Calcium plays essential roles in bone strength, muscle contraction, and cellular signaling, but it is not a primary factor in soft tissue wound healing. Hypocalcemia can cause neuromuscular excitability and cardiac arrhythmias — these are the clinically significant manifestations of calcium deficiency. It does not directly impair the collagen synthesis, immune function, or epithelialization that drive pressure ulcer healing. Calcium deficiency is not among the established nutritional causes of wound healing failure.

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | One nurse turns the torso while another turns the legs | ❌ NO |
| B | The head, shoulders, and hips must remain aligned | ✅ YES |
| C | Use of a drawsheet is contraindicated | ❌ NO |
| D | The nurse should position the legs bent during the turn | ❌ NO |

❌ **A — Incorrect:** This describes the opposite of correct log-rolling technique. Log-rolling requires the entire body to turn as a single, simultaneous unit. ❌ **D — Incorrect:** The legs must remain **straight and extended** during the rolling movement. Bending the hips causes pelvic rotation, which transmits rotational forces directly to the lumbar spine. Maintaining extended legs keeps the lumbar spine and pelvis in neutral alignment throughout the turn. A pillow may be placed between the knees once the patient reaches the side-lying position, but during the roll itself the legs stay straight.

### Question 86 — Skin Prep for Topical Ointment

**Topic:** Skin Integrity & Wound Care | **Concept:** Skin Preparation Before Topical Medication

|---|---|---|
| A | Clean with alcohol and let air dry | ❌ NO |
| B | Clean with soap and water, rinse, and pat dry | ✅ YES |
| C | Use sterile gloves for all topical medications | ❌ NO |
| D | Avoid cleaning the skin to preserve pH balance | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: B**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Alcohol strips the skin's natural lipid barrier (the acid mantle), causing dryness, irritation, and increased skin vulnerability. Applying topical ointment to intact skin or a non-sterile wound is a clean technique procedure requiring only **clean (non-sterile) gloves**. Using sterile gloves universally for all topical medications is unnecessary, clinically unjustified, and wasteful of resources. *Note: If applying medication to an actively draining sterile wound in a sterile field, sterile gloves would be appropriate — but this is not a universal requirement.*

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Skipping skin preparation to "preserve pH balance" is not a valid clinical rationale. The theoretical concern about disrupting the skin's acid mantle (pH 4.5–5.5) is addressed by using gentle, pH-balanced cleansers and avoiding harsh products — not by leaving old medication, debris, and bacteria on the skin surface. Old medication residue physically blocks the absorption of the new dose, and accumulated debris increases infection risk.

### Question 87 — Purulent Wound Drainage

**Topic:** Skin Integrity & Wound Care | **Concept:** Wound Assessment; Infection Signs

**Question:** A client has a wound with purulent drainage and increased warmth. What does this suggest?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Ischemia | ❌ NO |
| B | Dehiscence | ❌ NO |
| C | Wound infection | ✅ YES |
| D | Inflammation | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Wound ischemia results from inadequate blood supply to the wound tissue. It presents as pale, darkened, or black wound tissue; cool skin relative to surrounding areas; slow or absent healing; and pain disproportionate to wound size. Ischemia does not produce purulent drainage — purulent drainage requires an active bacterial infection with an intact enough immune response to generate pus (dead neutrophils, bacteria, cellular debris). *Note: Ischemia is a risk factor that predisposes wounds to infection, but it is not itself characterized by purulence.*

❌ **B — Incorrect:** Dehiscence is the mechanical separation or pulling apart of wound edges. It presents visually as a gap or opening between wound edges, sometimes with visible underlying tissue or organs. Dehiscence can result from poor wound closure, infection, excess tension on the wound, or premature removal of sutures. While dehiscence can create a pathway for infection, the finding of purulent drainage + warmth describes infection specifically — not wound edge separation. *Note: Evisceration (organ protrusion through a dehisced wound) is the most severe form of dehiscence and requires immediate emergency intervention.*

✅ **C — Correct:** Purulent drainage (thick, opaque, yellow/green/brown, foul-smelling) combined with **increased local warmth** are the hallmark signs of **wound infection**. Purulent material is composed of dead neutrophils, bacteria, and necrotic tissue — collectively called pus. Local warmth results from increased metabolic activity, vasodilation, and the inflammatory response to bacterial invasion. Together, these findings confirm infection requiring wound culture, wound care revision, and typically antibiotic therapy guided by culture results.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Inflammation is the **normal first phase** of wound healing — it produces redness, warmth, swelling, and pain (the classic four cardinal signs of inflammation). However, normal healing inflammation is: proportionate to wound size, transient (peaks days 1–3 post-injury), and associated with clear serous or serosanguineous drainage — not purulent drainage. When **purulent drainage** appears alongside warmth, the wound has progressed beyond normal healing inflammation into active bacterial infection. The presence of pus is the key distinguishing feature — it is never normal and always indicates infection.

### Question 88 — Polydipsia in Schizophrenia: Priority Concern

**Topic:** Fluid & Electrolyte Balance | **Concept:** Psychogenic Polydipsia; Dilutional Hyponatremia; Water Intoxication

**Question:** A client with schizophrenia drinks over 3 liters of water per day. What is the nurse's priority concern?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Risk for dehydration | ❌ NO |
| B | Risk for water intoxication | ✅ YES |
| C | Risk for urinary tract infection | ❌ NO |
| D | Risk for hypernatremia | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: B**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Dehydration results from inadequate fluid intake relative to losses. This patient is consuming more than 3 liters daily — the risk is from **excessive** water intake, not insufficient intake. Dehydration is the opposite of what this clinical picture presents.

✅ **B — Correct:** **Psychogenic polydipsia** — compulsive excessive water drinking without physiological thirst — is a recognized complication in psychiatric patients, particularly those with schizophrenia. Consuming >3 L/day can exceed the kidney's maximum free water excretion capacity, causing dilutional **hyponatremia** (water intoxication). The falling serum sodium and osmolality cause water to shift into cells, including brain cells. Cerebral edema produces a rapidly escalating syndrome: headache → confusion → decreased LOC → seizures → coma → death. Serum sodium monitoring, fluid restriction, and staff awareness are essential interventions.

❌ **C — Incorrect:** High fluid intake actually increases urinary frequency and flow — this mechanical flushing effect reduces bacterial colonization time in the bladder and typically **decreases** rather than increases UTI risk. UTI is not the priority concern with polydipsia.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Hypernatremia (Na⁺ > 145 mEq/L) results from excess sodium relative to water — from sodium gain or water loss. Drinking >3 L of water daily produces the opposite: **dilutional hyponatremia**, where excess water dilutes the serum sodium concentration. The patient is at risk for hypo-natremia, not hyper-natremia. *Note: Hypernatremia would be the concern if the patient were not drinking enough, had diabetes insipidus, or was losing excessive water through another route.*

### Question 89 — Stool: Requires Immediate Attention

**Topic:** Bowel Elimination | **Concept:** Stool Assessment; Abnormal Findings; Clinical Urgency

**Question:** Which stool assessment finding requires immediate attention?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Brown, soft-formed stool | ❌ NO |
| B | Clay-colored stool | ✅ YES |
| C | Semi-formed green stool after antibiotics | ❌ NO |
| D | Loose stool after iron supplement | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: B**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Brown, soft-formed stool is **normal**. The brown color comes from urobilinogen (a bilirubin breakdown product), and the soft, formed consistency reflects normal intestinal transit and water absorption. This finding requires no intervention.

✅ **B — Correct:** Clay-colored (pale, gray, or putty-white) stool indicates the **absence of bile** from the intestinal contents. Antibiotics alter the gut microbiome by reducing normal bacterial flora, which changes bile metabolism and fermentation in the colon, producing green-tinted stool. This is transient and resolves as the microbiome normalizes after antibiotics are completed. *Note: The nurse should still monitor for *C. difficile* infection — profuse watery diarrhea with cramping and fever after antibiotics would require immediate testing and contact precautions.*

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Loose or dark-green/black stools are well-known, expected side effects of iron supplementation. Iron salts irritate the GI mucosa and alter stool color and consistency. Patients should be counseled about this during medication teaching. *Note: Black, tarry stool (melena) from upper GI bleeding can be confused with iron-induced black stool — melena has a distinctively sticky, tar-like consistency and foul odor, and would require immediate attention. Iron-related dark stools are expected and should be distinguished from this.*

### Question 90 — Diabetic Foot Care Teaching

**Topic:** Hygiene & Skin Care | **Concept:** Diabetic Foot Care; Peripheral Neuropathy; Teaching Correct vs. Incorrect Practices

**Question:** A nurse is teaching a diabetic client about foot care. Which instruction is correct?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Cut toenails straight across | ✅ YES |
| B | Soak feet daily in hot water | ❌ NO |
| C | Apply lotion between the toes | ❌ NO |
| D | Walk barefoot at home for circulation | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: A**

**Rationales:**

✅ **A — Correct:** Cutting toenails **straight across** prevents ingrown toenails. When nails are cut in a curved shape following the toe contour, the corners of the nail can dig into the surrounding soft tissue, creating a portal for infection. In a patient with diabetic neuropathy, an ingrown nail may become seriously infected before being detected because pain — the normal warning signal — is absent or impaired.

Impaired sensation prevents the patient from accurately detecting water temperature — water that feels "warm" to their insensate foot may actually be hot enough to cause scalds. This promotes maceration and fungal infection (tinea pedis), which can then serve as an entry point for bacterial secondary infection. Moisture-related skin breakdown between toes is a recognized starting point for diabetic foot ulceration. Lotion should be applied to the dorsum (top) and plantar (sole) surfaces but deliberately avoided between the toes.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Walking barefoot poses a significant injury risk for patients who cannot feel their feet. Sharp objects, rough surfaces, splinters, and thermal surfaces (hot sand, pavement, bathroom floors) can cause wounds that go completely undetected until infection is advanced. Patients must wear properly-fitted, closed-toe shoes or slippers at all times — even indoors. *Note: The "for circulation" rationale is misguided — ambulation does improve circulation, but it must be done in appropriate protective footwear.*

### Question 94 — Preventing Medication Errors

**Topic:** Medication Administration | **Concept:** Medication Safety; Error Prevention; Five Rights

**Question:** Which strategy helps prevent future medication errors?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Never question a provider's order | ❌ NO |
| B | Avoid double-checking meds when rushed | ❌ NO |
| C | Follow the five rights of medication administration | ✅ YES |
| D | Skip documenting verbal orders | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Questioning provider orders is not only acceptable — it is a **professional and legal obligation** when an order appears unclear, potentially harmful, or inconsistent with the patient's clinical picture. Nurses who administer medications they believed were incorrect without questioning them bear legal and professional accountability for the outcome. Blind deference to authority has contributed to numerous catastrophic medication errors. "If in doubt, check it out" is a foundational safety principle.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** Time pressure is one of the most common contributing factors to medication errors — rushing leads to missed verification steps, distraction, and incorrect assumptions. These structured checks intercept errors before they reach the patient — wrong patient identification, incorrect medication pulled, miscalculated dose, inappropriate route, or wrong timing are all caught by the five rights when applied rigorously. This is the foundational, universally recognized medication safety framework.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** All verbal orders must be **documented** using a read-back process (nurse repeats the order back to the provider for confirmation) and signed by the provider within the facility's required timeframe. Failure to document verbal orders creates dangerous gaps: no written record of what was ordered, no ability to verify the order later, risk of double dosing, and no legal protection for the nurse. Documentation of verbal orders is not optional — it is a safety and legal requirement.

### Question 97 — Ice Pack After Abdominal Surgery

**Topic:** Pain Management | **Concept:** Cold Therapy; Patient Teaching; Cold vs. Heat Effects

**Question:** A client recovering from abdominal surgery asks for an ice pack. Which teaching point is accurate?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | "Ice reduces inflammation and numbs pain." | ✅ YES |
| B | "Ice will prevent infection." | ❌ NO |
| C | "Ice should be left on for 60 minutes." | ❌ NO |
| D | "Ice will loosen tight muscles." | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: A**

**Rationales:**

✅ **A — Correct:** Cold therapy works through two primary mechanisms: **vasoconstriction** (reduces blood flow to the area → decreases inflammatory mediator delivery, reduces swelling and edema) and **local analgesia** (cold temperatures reduce nerve conduction velocity → numbs the area → reduces pain signal transmission). For post-operative patients, cold therapy applied to the surgical site can reduce acute inflammatory pain and swelling. This is a non-pharmacological independent intervention within the nurse's scope.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** Ice does not prevent infection. Infection is caused by bacterial (or other pathogen) contamination of a wound — it is prevented through sterile technique, hand hygiene, wound care, and appropriate antibiotic prophylaxis when indicated. Cold temperatures can actually **impair** immune function locally by reducing neutrophil and macrophage activity in the cooled area. Ice has no antimicrobial properties.

❌ **C — Incorrect:** Ice should be applied for a **maximum of 20 minutes** at a time, followed by a period off before reapplication. Prolonged application beyond 20 minutes causes rebound vasodilation (the blood vessels paradoxically dilate to prevent frostbite), reducing the therapeutic anti-inflammatory effect. More critically, prolonged ice application can cause frostbite, ice burns, and tissue damage — particularly through thin or compromised skin. 60 minutes is far too long and represents a patient harm risk.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Loosening tight muscles is the effect of **heat**, not cold. Heat promotes vasodilation, increases tissue extensibility, relaxes muscle spasm, and reduces tension in chronically tight muscles — this is why heating pads are used for back muscle pain and muscle cramps. Cold has the opposite effect — it can temporarily increase muscle stiffness and is used for acute injuries and inflammation, not muscle relaxation.

### Question 99 — ROM in Client with Left-Sided Weakness

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Perform exercises only on the unaffected side | ❌ NO |
| B | Encourage the client to complete all movements independently | ❌ NO |
| C | Support joints while moving the limb slowly | ✅ YES |
| D | Hyperextend joints for maximal benefit | ❌ NO |

### Question 100 — Home NGT Safety Teaching

**Topic:** Nutrition & Diet Therapy | **Concept:** NG Tube Home Care; Aspiration Prevention Teaching

**Question:** What teaching should be included regarding safety and NGT care at home?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Wash hands only after handling the NGT | ❌ NO |
| B | Check tube placement with auscultation | ❌ NO |
| C | Tape the tube loosely to prevent pressure sores | ❌ NO |
| D | Elevate head of bed at least 30° during and after feedings | ✅ YES |

❌ **C — Incorrect:** The NGT must be taped **securely** to prevent dislodgement — not loosely. A loosely taped tube can be inadvertently pulled out by the patient during movement, shifting position, or suctioning, requiring re-insertion. Accidental dislodgement of a feeding tube with a feeding infusing can cause aspiration. *Note: The tape should be positioned carefully to avoid prolonged pressure on the same skin area, and the skin should be inspected regularly — but the tube must be securely anchored.*

✅ **D — Correct:** Maintaining the HOB at **at least 30°** during feedings and for at least 30–60 minutes after feedings is the most critical aspiration prevention teaching for home NGT patients. This position uses gravity to keep gastric contents in the stomach, reduces reflux to the esophagus, and significantly lowers aspiration risk. Many serious complications of home enteral feeding — aspiration pneumonia — can be prevented with consistent adherence to this positioning requirement. This is the most important safety teaching point.

| C | Apply a non-rebreather mask | ✅ YES |
| D | Suction the airway | ❌ NO |

### Question 105 — Trach Suction Catheter Disposal

### Question 107 — Terminal Client: DNR Request

**Topic:** Legal & Ethical Concepts | **Concept:** Advance Directives; DNR Orders; Nursing Responsibilities

**Question:** A terminal client states, "I don't want CPR if my heart stops." What should the nurse do next?

|---|---|---|
| A | Inform the provider to write a DNR | ✅ YES |
| B | Tell the client this decision must be made by family | ❌ NO |
| C | Reassure them CPR is always provided | ❌ NO |
| D | Document the statement and do nothing else | ❌ NO |

**Rationales:**

✅ **A — Correct:** The nurse must **notify the healthcare provider** so that a formal DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) order can be written in the medical record. The nurse's role is to: (1) document the patient's statement, (2) notify the provider of the patient's expressed wish, and (3) advocate for the patient's right to make this autonomous decision. More importantly, this response dismisses the patient's expressed wishes and provides misinformation that could cause the patient to lose trust in the healthcare team and feel their autonomy is being ignored.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Documenting the statement and taking no further action fails the patient. Documentation is necessary — but it is not sufficient. The patient has expressed a specific end-of-life wish that requires clinical action: provider notification and order writing. A documented patient statement without an accompanying provider order has no legal force — if the patient's heart stops before the order is written, CPR will be performed regardless of what was documented without an order.

### Question 115 — Chronic Insomnia: Best Assessment Question

**Topic:** Sleep | **Concept:** Sleep Assessment; Insomnia History; Sleep Hygiene Evaluation

**Question:** A client with chronic insomnia presents for evaluation. Which assessment question is most appropriate?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | "Do you dream often?" | ❌ NO |
| B | "Have you changed your diet recently?" | ❌ NO |
| C | "What is your normal bedtime routine?" | ✅ YES |
| D | "How many hours do you sleep on weekends?" | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Frequency of dreaming provides minimal actionable clinical information for a patient with chronic insomnia. Dreaming occurs during REM sleep — patients who report dreaming may be sleeping, but the presence or absence of dreams does not identify the cause of insomnia or guide treatment. This question does not help the nurse assess sleep hygiene, behavioral factors, or underlying causes.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** Dietary changes may have some relevance to sleep (caffeine content, large late-night meals), but "have you changed your diet recently?" is too vague and narrow to serve as the best opening assessment question. It focuses on a single potential contributing factor rather than opening a comprehensive assessment of sleep patterns and behaviors.

✅ **C — Correct:** Assessing the client's **bedtime routine** is the most comprehensive and clinically productive initial question for a chronic insomnia evaluation. It opens the conversation to explore: What time does the patient go to bed and attempt sleep? What activities precede bedtime (screen time, exercise, alcohol, caffeine)? Is the environment conducive to sleep? Does the patient associate the bed with wakefulness rather than sleep? Are there psychological factors (worry, racing thoughts) at bedtime? This single question provides a framework for identifying modifiable sleep hygiene factors that are the targets of evidence-based insomnia treatment (CBT-I — Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia).

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Weekend sleep duration provides only a narrow data point. Many insomnia patients sleep differently on weekends due to variable schedules, and weekend "catch-up sleep" can itself perpetuate insomnia by disrupting the circadian rhythm. While weekend vs. weekday sleep comparison may be useful supplementary information, it is not the most informative opening question — it provides one data point rather than opening a holistic assessment of sleep habits.

### Question 116 — Spanish-Speaking Confused Client

**Topic:** Communication & Therapeutic Relationships | **Concept:** Language Barriers; Professional Medical Interpreter

**Question:** A nurse is caring for a Spanish-speaking client who appears confused. What is the priority action?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Use a family member to translate | ❌ NO |
| B | Speak louder and slower | ❌ NO |
| C | Use a professional medical interpreter | ✅ YES |
| D | Point to pictures and use gestures | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Using family members as interpreters is problematic in clinical settings for multiple reasons: (1) they may lack medical terminology in either language, leading to mistranslation of critical information; (2) they may consciously or unconsciously filter, alter, or embellish what the patient says — particularly about sensitive topics; (3) their presence may inhibit the patient from disclosing symptoms, behaviors, or concerns they would share privately with the healthcare team; (4) if the patient is confused, having family interpret adds another layer of potential miscommunication. Professional interpreters are required by CLAS Standards (Culturally and Linguistically Appropriate Services) and Joint Commission standards.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** Speaking more loudly does not improve language comprehension. A patient who does not speak English does not have a hearing problem — they have a language barrier. Increasing volume communicates only frustration; it does not make English words comprehensible to a non-English speaker. This response is ineffective and can come across as condescending.

✅ **C — Correct:** A **professional medical interpreter** — whether in person, via telephone, or via video — is the required standard of care when a language barrier exists. Professional interpreters have training in medical terminology in both languages, adherence to confidentiality standards, skills in accurate bidirectional interpretation, and the professional objectivity that family members lack. For a confused patient, accurate interpretation of symptoms, history, and clinical questions is particularly critical — errors in communication with a confused patient can lead to misdiagnosis and inappropriate treatment.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Pictures and gestures (visual communication aids) can be useful supplements for conveying very basic information — pointing to a pain scale, indicating a body part. However, they cannot substitute for nuanced clinical communication: obtaining a detailed history, explaining a diagnosis, discussing treatment options, or obtaining meaningful informed consent. For a patient who is also confused (potentially indicating delirium or another neurological issue), visual communication without linguistic assessment is insufficient for safe clinical care.

### Question 117 — Opioid PCA: Respiratory Depression

**Topic:** Pain Management | **Concept:** PCA Safety; Opioid Toxicity; Respiratory Depression; Priority Intervention

**Question:** A nurse notes shallow respirations and excessive drowsiness in a client on opioids via PCA. What is the priority action?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Continue to monitor | ❌ NO |
| B | Increase oxygen via nasal cannula | ❌ NO |
| C | Stop the PCA pump and notify the provider | ✅ YES |
| D | Document the findings in the MAR | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Continuing to monitor without intervention when a patient is showing signs of opioid-induced respiratory depression is a patient safety failure. Shallow respirations and excessive drowsiness represent clinically significant opioid toxicity — if unaddressed, these can progress to respiratory arrest. The nurse must intervene immediately, not observe and wait.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** Applying oxygen via nasal cannula may temporarily support oxygenation by providing supplemental inspired oxygen, but it does not address the cause of the problem — ongoing opioid delivery through the PCA. Oxygen alone will not reverse opioid toxicity. If the PCA continues infusing opioid while oxygen is added, the respiratory depression will deepen despite the supplemental oxygen. The PCA must be stopped first. *Note: Oxygen application is an appropriate concurrent action but is not the priority first step.*

✅ **C — Correct:** The **first priority** is to **stop the PCA pump** — halt further opioid delivery immediately. Shallow respirations and excessive drowsiness in a PCA patient indicate opioid-induced respiratory depression, a life-threatening emergency. Every additional dose of opioid worsens the respiratory depression. After stopping the pump, the nurse immediately notifies the provider, who may order naloxone (opioid reversal), orders monitoring, and assesses for further intervention. Supplemental oxygen should be applied concurrently.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Documentation is required — but it is never the priority action in an acute clinical emergency. Documenting findings while a patient is experiencing respiratory depression from opioid toxicity delays the critical interventions that protect the patient's life. Document after the immediate clinical response is initiated.

### Question 118 — Evaluating Teaching Effectiveness

**Topic:** Patient Education | **Concept:** Evaluating Patient Understanding; Return Demonstration; Teach-Back

**Question:** A nurse evaluates the effectiveness of a teaching plan. Which outcome best demonstrates understanding?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Client nods during explanation | ❌ NO |
| B | Client says "I understand" | ❌ NO |
| C | Client correctly demonstrates return demonstration | ✅ YES |
| D | Client says they'll look it up online later | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Nodding during explanation is passive, non-verbal behavior that indicates attention (or social compliance) — not comprehension. Patients nod for many reasons unrelated to understanding: they may be following social norms of appearing attentive, they may be too embarrassed to indicate they don't understand, or they may be nodding along without processing the content. Nodding provides no evidence of learning.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** "I understand" is a self-assessment of comprehension that has been consistently shown to be unreliable. Patients who say "I understand" frequently cannot demonstrate what they claim to understand when tested. This verbal confirmation is socially satisfying but clinically meaningless without verification. The teach-back method was developed specifically to replace this type of unverified verbal response.

✅ **C — Correct:** **Return demonstration** — having the patient correctly perform the skill or explain the process back in their own words — is the gold standard for evaluating teaching effectiveness. It tests actual comprehension and skill acquisition, not self-reported understanding. Correct performance proves the patient has internalized the teaching to the level of independent application. For psychomotor skills (insulin injection, wound care, catheter management), return demonstration is the only reliable evaluation method.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Saying they'll look it up online later defers learning and demonstrates that no learning has yet occurred in the teaching session. It also introduces the risk that the patient will find inaccurate or inapplicable information online. A patient who plans to independently research what they were just taught did not successfully receive or retain the instruction. The teaching session has not achieved its goal.

### Question 119 — Advance Directive vs. Family Demand

**Topic:** Legal & Ethical Concepts | **Concept:** Advance Directives; Patient Advocacy; Autonomy; Legal Precedence

**Question:** The client's advance directive requests no tube feedings, but the family demands feeding be started. What is the nurse's ethical responsibility?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Follow the family's wishes | ❌ NO |
| B | Begin feeding and document the conflict | ❌ NO |
| C | Advocate for the client's expressed wishes | ✅ YES |
| D | Defer to the provider's decision | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Following the family's wishes over a valid advance directive violates the patient's legal rights and the ethical principle of autonomy. The advance directive represents the patient's own voice — their expressed wishes for their care documented while competent. Family members, however well-intentioned, do not have the legal authority to override a patient's valid advance directive.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** Beginning tube feedings against the patient's documented wishes — even while documenting the conflict — is a violation of the patient's rights. Documenting a conflict does not justify proceeding with an intervention the patient has explicitly refused. This action exposes the nurse and institution to serious legal liability and violates the patient's autonomy and the legal standing of the advance directive.

✅ **C — Correct:** The nurse's ethical and legal responsibility is to **advocate for the client's expressed wishes** — specifically, to uphold the advance directive. The advance directive is a legal document reflecting the patient's autonomous decision while they were competent to make it. Its legal authority supersedes family preference. The nurse must: decline to initiate tube feeding against the directive, explain the legal standing of the document to the family, notify the provider and nursing supervisor, and if necessary involve the ethics committee to support the patient's documented wishes.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Deferring entirely to the provider's decision without nursing advocacy abdicates the nurse's professional responsibility as a patient advocate. If the provider decides to honor the family's request over the advance directive, the nurse is not obligated to comply with an order that violates the patient's legal documents. The nurse has an independent professional obligation to advocate for the patient and may refuse to implement a care order that directly contradicts the patient's legal advance directive while escalating through appropriate channels (supervisor, ethics committee, risk management).

### Question 120 — Insulin Teaching: Most Effective Method

**Topic:** Patient Education | **Concept:** Teaching Methods; Psychomotor Skill Learning; Return Demonstration

**Question:** Which strategy is most effective when teaching a newly diagnosed diabetic client about insulin?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Reading the manufacturer's pamphlet | ❌ NO |
| B | Watching a video independently | ❌ NO |
| C | Return demonstration using an orange | ✅ YES |
| D | Reviewing the sliding scale chart alone | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Manufacturer pamphlets provide written reference information but require literacy, health literacy, and the ability to translate written instructions into physical actions. Reading about how to inject insulin does not develop the psychomotor skill of actually performing the injection. Pamphlets are useful as supplemental reference material — not as the primary teaching method for a skill that requires hand-eye coordination and physical practice.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** Watching a video provides visual modeling of the skill — this is more effective than reading alone. However, it remains a passive learning method: the patient observes but does not practice. Research consistently shows that passive observation does not produce the same skill acquisition, confidence, or retention as active practice. Video is a useful adjunct, not the primary teaching tool.

✅ **C — Correct:** **Return demonstration using a practice object** (traditionally an orange — its texture approximates skin and allows safe needle practice) is the most effective method for teaching insulin injection. It incorporates: (1) **active, hands-on practice** of the psychomotor skill, (2) **immediate feedback** from the nurse on technique, (3) **repetition** to build muscle memory and confidence, and (4) **evaluation** of whether the patient can actually perform the skill independently. Confidence is a critical component — newly diagnosed patients often have significant needle anxiety, and successful practice builds the self-efficacy needed for daily adherence.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Reviewing a sliding scale chart provides cognitive information about when and how much insulin to administer based on blood glucose — this is important knowledge. However, it is a cognitive/decision-making skill, not a psychomotor skill. Knowing when to inject does not teach how to inject. Both components of insulin self-management need to be taught, but for the **most effective** overall teaching strategy, return demonstration of the injection technique is the correct answer.

### Question 121 — Care Plan Goal: Confused Client

**Topic:** Nursing Process & Clinical Judgment | **Concept:** Goal Setting; Realistic and Achievable Goals; Confused Patient

**Question:** A nurse is developing a care plan for a confused client. Which goal is most appropriate?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Client will sleep 8 hours per night | ❌ NO |
| B | Client will remain oriented ×3 at all times | ❌ NO |
| C | Client will be free from injury during hospitalization | ✅ YES |
| D | Client will ambulate independently | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Guaranteeing 8 hours of uninterrupted sleep for a hospitalized patient — particularly one who is confused and may have altered sleep-wake cycles — is not realistic. Hospital environments are inherently disruptive (noise, procedures, vital sign checks, medication schedules), and confusion itself often involves sleep-wake cycle disturbance (sundowning, delirium). This goal is not achievable and sets both the patient and the care team up for failure.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** "Oriented ×3 at all times" is not a realistic goal for a client who is **currently confused**. Confusion may be caused by delirium, dementia, metabolic disturbance, or other conditions that are not immediately correctable. Demanding full orientation at all times as a care goal is inappropriate because: (1) it may not be achievable given the underlying cause; (2) "at all times" creates an impossibly constant standard; (3) failure to meet this goal does not mean the care plan failed — it may simply reflect the natural course of the condition.

✅ **C — Correct:** "Free from injury during hospitalization" is the most **realistic, achievable, and appropriate** goal for a confused client. It focuses on the primary safety concern — confusion creates high fall and injury risk — and is actionable through specific, measurable nursing interventions (low bed, non-skid footwear, fall alarm, bed in lowest position, 1:1 supervision, environment modification). This goal can be evaluated objectively (did the patient sustain an injury?) and drives a clear prevention-focused plan of care.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Independent ambulation as a goal for a confused client without fall risk assessment or interventional support is unsafe. A confused patient attempting to ambulate independently is at high risk for falls. If ambulation is clinically appropriate, the goal should specify safe assisted ambulation with nursing support — not independent ambulation, which would require intact cognition, stable gait, and the ability to call for help when needed.

### Question 122 — Cultural Sensitivity: Statement Needing Correction

**Topic:** Communication & Therapeutic Relationships | **Concept:** Cultural Competence; Professional Medical Interpreters; Teaching

**Question:** During a cultural sensitivity educational session, which statement by the nurse needs correction?

| # | Statement | Needs Correction? |
|---|---|---|
| A | "Every culture views health differently." | ❌ NO — correct |
| B | "Using family as translators helps clients feel supported." | ✅ YES — needs correction |
| C | "Religion may affect end-of-life decisions." | ❌ NO — correct |
| D | "Always ask clients about cultural preferences." | ❌ NO — correct |

**Correct answer: B** *(the statement that needs correction)*

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Correct statement (no correction needed):** Every culture does view health, illness, death, and the role of medicine differently. Cultural beliefs about the etiology of illness, the role of healers, the value of technology-driven care, dietary practices, and decision-making processes vary widely across cultural groups. This is a foundational principle of culturally competent care.

✅ **B — Needs correction:** This statement is problematic despite appearing supportive. While family **presence** can be emotionally supportive, using family members as **medical interpreters** is not best practice for several reasons: (1) family members may not have accurate medical vocabulary in either language, leading to dangerous mistranslation; (2) they may filter or alter information to protect the patient emotionally; (3) the patient may withhold sensitive information in front of family (domestic violence, substance use, sexual health concerns); (4) family members may not accurately convey bad news; (5) they may consciously or unconsciously advocate for their own preferences rather than the patient's. **Professional medical interpreters** are the required standard. Family presence during care is encouraged — family as translators is not.

❌ **C — Correct statement (no correction needed):** Religious beliefs profoundly influence end-of-life decision-making for many patients — including views on life support, organ donation, autopsy, blood transfusion, and the moral weight of a natural death vs. prolonged medical intervention. Acknowledging religion's role in these decisions is culturally competent and essential for individualized care.

❌ **D — Correct statement (no correction needed):** Asking patients about their cultural preferences — rather than assuming or applying generalizations — is the cornerstone of individualized cultural care. Cultural humility involves approaching each patient as an individual who may or may not conform to cultural norms associated with their background. Asking rather than assuming respects individual variation within cultural groups.

### Question 123 — ALS Client: Open-Ended Communication

**Topic:** Communication & Therapeutic Relationships | **Concept:** Open-Ended Questions; Therapeutic Communication; Existential Distress

**Question:** A client newly diagnosed with ALS states, "I don't see any reason to keep trying anymore." Which response best demonstrates open-ended therapeutic communication?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | "You have many treatment options that can help slow the disease." | ❌ NO |
| B | "Why do you feel like there is no reason to continue?" | ❌ NO |
| C | "Describe what you are experiencing since receiving this diagnosis." | ✅ YES |
| D | "You shouldn't give up before exploring all available resources." | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Immediately shifting to information about treatment options redirects the conversation from the patient's emotional expression to medical management. The patient is communicating existential distress — hopelessness in the face of a devastating diagnosis. Jumping to "there are options" dismisses this distress and implies the patient's feelings are a problem to be solved with information rather than an experience to be acknowledged and explored. This is a premature, content-focused response when the patient needs emotional acknowledgment first.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** "Why do you feel that way?" sounds open-ended but is problematic in this context. "Why" questions can feel interrogative and accusatory — they place the patient in a position of having to justify or defend their emotional state. For a patient expressing hopelessness or passive suicidal ideation, a "why" question can feel confrontational. It also implies the feeling is unreasonable or needs explanation. More importantly, it closes off the full description of experience that a broader invitation ("describe what you are experiencing") would open.

✅ **C — Correct:** "Describe what you are experiencing since receiving this diagnosis" is the most therapeutically effective response because it: (1) is **open-ended** — it invites a full, unrestricted description rather than a specific answer; (2) is **non-judgmental** — it does not evaluate, challenge, or redirect the patient's feelings; (3) **validates** the patient's experience by implicitly acknowledging that a diagnosis like ALS produces a complex and significant emotional response; (4) allows the nurse to assess the nature and depth of the distress (grief? passive hopelessness? active suicidal ideation?), which guides subsequent clinical decisions. This response exemplifies therapeutic presence and communication.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** "You shouldn't give up before exploring all available resources" is prescriptive and dismissive. It tells the patient what they should do (not give up), implies judgment about their current stance, and redirects to practical action before acknowledging the emotional experience. This response, like option A, substitutes problem-solving for empathic presence — the opposite of what a patient expressing hopelessness needs in the moment.

### Question 124 — Unmet Care Plan Goals: Next Step

**Topic:** Nursing Process & Clinical Judgment | **Concept:** Evaluation; Unmet Goals; Care Plan Revision

**Question:** A nurse reviewing goals for a client notes that two were unmet. What is the correct next step?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | Delete the goals from the plan of care | ❌ NO |
| B | Continue the same interventions | ❌ NO |
| C | Modify interventions and reset the timeline | ✅ YES |
| D | Ask the client to write their own plan | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: C**

**Rationales:**

❌ **A — Incorrect:** Unmet goals must **never be deleted** from the care plan. An unmet goal represents an ongoing, unresolved clinical problem. Deleting it removes the documentation of a known problem, creates a false impression that the patient's needs have been addressed, and eliminates the accountability that the care plan provides. Unmet goals remain on the care plan, active and visible, until they are achieved or the clinical situation changes (e.g., the patient is discharged or the problem resolves through a different pathway).

❌ **B — Incorrect:** Continuing the same interventions when a goal has not been met repeats an approach that has already been demonstrated to be ineffective. Albert Einstein's often-cited definition of insanity applies here: doing the same thing repeatedly while expecting a different result. The Evaluation phase of the nursing process requires the nurse to assess *why* the goal was not met — was the diagnosis correct? Were the interventions appropriate? Was there a barrier? — and then modify the plan accordingly.

✅ **C — Correct:** **Modifying the interventions and resetting the timeline** is the evidence-based response to unmet goals in nursing care planning. The Evaluation phase (the E of ADPIE) specifically requires: (1) determining whether goals were met, partially met, or not met; (2) identifying reasons for unmet goals (wrong diagnosis? insufficient interventions? patient non-adherence? barriers not previously identified?); (3) revising the plan — changing interventions, adding new ones, or adjusting the goal timeline; and (4) continuing through the nursing process cycle until the goal is achieved.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** While collaborative care planning that incorporates patient preferences and patient-identified priorities is best practice, asking the patient to write their own care plan is inappropriate and unrealistic. The care plan is a professional clinical document that requires nursing knowledge, clinical judgment, and integration of assessment data, diagnoses, interventions, and outcome criteria. Patients contribute to care planning through shared decision-making — they do not independently author clinical care plans.

### Question 125 — Clear Liquid Diet: Popsicle

**Topic:** Nutrition & Diet Therapy | **Concept:** Clear Liquid Diet; Allowed Items; Patient Teaching

**Question:** A client on a clear liquid diet asks if they can have a popsicle. What is the nurse's best response?

| # | Option | Correct? |
|---|---|---|
| A | "Only fruit-flavored popsicles without pulp are allowed." | ✅ YES |
| B | "Popsicles are not allowed on a clear liquid diet." | ❌ NO |
| C | "You may have any popsicles as long as they are sugar-free." | ❌ NO |
| D | "Popsicles are allowed only after your next meal." | ❌ NO |

**Correct answer: A**

**Rationales:**

✅ **A — Correct:** Clear, **fruit-flavored popsicles** that are **transparent** (containing no cream, dairy, or fruit pulp) are permitted on a clear liquid diet. The criteria for clear liquid items are: transparency (can see through them), liquid at room temperature, no residue in the GI tract. A clear fruit popsicle meets all criteria when it: is made with clear juice or flavored water, contains no cream or milk, and contains no fruit pulp or solid pieces. These popsicles provide fluid, simple carbohydrates, and electrolytes while meeting the GI rest requirements of the diet.

❌ **B — Incorrect:** This response is too broad and inaccurate. Clear popsicles (transparent, no dairy, no pulp) ARE permitted on a clear liquid diet. Telling the patient that all popsicles are prohibited denies a valid dietary option and may cause unnecessary frustration or non-compliance when the patient seeks food. The nurse must know the specific criteria and communicate them accurately — distinguishing between allowed and prohibited types.

❌ **C — Incorrect:** Sugar-free status is **not** the determining criterion for clear liquid diet compliance. The relevant criteria are: transparency, liquid state, and absence of residue — not sugar content. A sugar-free popsicle made with cream or milk (e.g., a sugar-free creamsicle) would still be prohibited because it is opaque and contains dairy. Conversely, a regular-sugar fruit popsicle with no cream or pulp is permitted. The sugar content is irrelevant to the diet classification.

❌ **D — Incorrect:** Timing restrictions based on meals do not apply to clear liquid diets. A clear liquid diet allows the permitted items at any time — there is no "after your next meal" restriction. This response is fabricated, inaccurate, and would confuse the patient. Dietary restrictions are based on what foods are permitted, not when they can be consumed within the diet category.
